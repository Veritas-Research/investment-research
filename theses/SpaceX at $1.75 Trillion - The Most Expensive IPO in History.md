# SpaceX at $1.75 Trillion: The Most Expensive IPO in History, and Why I'm Not Buying

**Author:** Mark A, Veritas Research
**Published:** June 9, 2026
**Series:** Standalone valuation note (companion to The Memory Play series)
**Repository:** github.com/Veritas-Research/investment-research

*One-line orientation: the most hyped Initial Public Offering (IPO) of our lifetime is also the most expensive ever sold, and once you do the simple math, passing on it is not caution, it is arithmetic.*

First-use definitions: Initial Public Offering (IPO), Price-to-Sales ratio (P/S), Price-to-Earnings ratio (P/E), Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), Discounted Cash Flow (DCF), Artificial Intelligence (AI), Total Addressable Market (TAM).

---

## Executive Summary (2-Minute Version)

- **Context:** SpaceX goes public on June 12 at **$135 per share**, a valuation of about **$1.75 trillion**. It is the largest IPO in history by money raised.
- **What's happening:** The hype says deal of a lifetime. The math says the most expensive large debut ever, about **94 times sales**, for a company that **lost $4.9 billion** last year and whose only profitable piece is Starlink.
- **What it would take:** To make today's price merely fair, SpaceX would have to grow roughly **60-fold to about $1.1 trillion in revenue by 2035**, a path no company has ever walked. Even the most generous independent expert (Aswath Damodaran) says you are overpaying by about **a quarter**, and his number leans on an AI unit (xAI) that even he is nervous about. Strip that out and you are overpaying by closer to **40% or more**.
- **The structure:** Less than a quarter of the money raised actually funds new growth, a director's firm sits on the other side of a **$20 billion related-party deal** the company's own books treat as debt, and the lock-up lets early backers start selling before you can.
- **My position:** **Passing.** Not shorting, not buying. If you believe the AI story, and I do, there are better-priced ways to own it.
- **Key risk to my view:** Overpriced is not the same as "falls tomorrow." A great story and Elon Musk's track record can hold a price up far longer than the math says it should.

# Part 1: Why I'm writing this

Over the past couple of weeks, more people have asked me about the SpaceX Initial Public Offering (IPO) than about any stock I can remember. Friends, family, people who have never bought a single share in their lives. And I kept giving the same answer, over and over, until I realized the answer deserved more than a text message. So I wrote it down, with the actual numbers, so anyone can read it and decide for themselves.

Here is the short version of what I have been telling them. This is probably one of the biggest stock-market events of our lifetime, and I mean that in both the best and the worst way. The best, because SpaceX is a genuinely extraordinary company. The worst, because at this price it is one of the poorest risk-to-reward trades I have seen offered to ordinary investors in years. Not because the rockets do not work. Because the math does not.

I would rather put my reasoning on the record, publicly and accountably, than just say "trust me." So let me show you why, in plain numbers, and then show you where I would put the same dollar instead. Because here is the part the hype machine never mentions: a bet is never good or bad on its own. It is good or bad compared to what else you could do with the money.

The facts of the deal first. SpaceX prices after the close on June 11 and starts trading June 12 on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX, at **$135 per share**, for a valuation of about **$1.75 trillion** (Fortune pegs the fully diluted figure slightly higher, around $1.77 trillion). It is raising roughly **$75 billion**, up to about **$86 billion** if the bankers exercise their full option. That is the **largest IPO in history by money raised**, roughly three times the prior record (Alibaba in 2014). To be precise, it is not the most valuable company ever to list, Saudi Aramco was larger at its debut. It is the biggest capital raise ever, and the most expensive ever on the measures that matter below.

So far that is just "big." Big is not the problem. The price is the problem.

---

# Part 2: You are paying 94 dollars for 1 dollar, and the company loses money

Here is the cleanest way to see it.

In 2025 SpaceX collected about **$18.7 billion** in sales (now disclosed in its IPO prospectus, so this is an audited figure, not a guess). At a $1.75 trillion price tag, you are paying about **$94 for every $1 of sales** the company made in 2025. That measure is called Price-to-Sales, and 94 is not a typo. I use it because the other common yardstick, Price-to-Earnings, cannot be calculated here: SpaceX has no profits to divide by.

For comparison:

| Company | Roughly what investors paid per $1 of sales at debut |
| :-- | :-- |
| Google (2004 IPO) | about $10 |
| Palantir (2020 direct listing) | about $15 |
| **SpaceX (2026 IPO)** | **about $94** |

You are being asked to pay roughly nine times the sales multiple Google's buyers paid, and Google was already profitable.

And that is the second problem. SpaceX as a whole **lost about $4.9 billion in 2025**. Only one piece of it, the Starlink satellite-internet business, makes money. The launch business and the new AI unit do not. So the company is not unprofitable because the business is broken. It loses money because it pours Starlink's profits, and a great deal of borrowed money, straight back into Starship and AI. That can be smart. But it means you are not buying earnings today. You are buying a story and trusting the earnings show up later.

Think of it like buying a lemonade stand. A normal stand that makes $1 of profit might sell for $15 or $20. This stand makes no net profit yet (its one good product funds the rest), and the asking price is $94 for every $1 of lemonade it sold last year, on the promise that it one day becomes Coca-Cola. SpaceX is a far better lemonade stand than most, a near-monopoly in launch and a fast-growing Starlink. But look at what "becoming Coca-Cola" actually requires here.

---

# Part 3: The growth required has never happened. Not once.

David Trainer of the research firm New Constructs ran the numbers (reported by Fortune on June 6). To make today's price merely fair, meaning you earn a modest 10% a year over the next decade, SpaceX would need to reach about **$1.1 trillion in revenue by 2035**.

Let me put that in human terms.

- Today: about **$18.7 billion** in sales a year.
- Required by 2035: about **$1.1 trillion** a year.
- That is roughly **60 times bigger in ten years**, which means growing sales about 50% every single year, for ten years straight, without stumbling.

And the single most absurd part: in the final year of that climb, sales would have to jump by about **$360 billion in one year**.

For scale, the fastest-growing large company in the world right now, Nvidia, added about **$85 billion** in sales in its best year ever (from roughly $130 billion to $216 billion). SpaceX would need to add more than four times that, in a single year, after it is already gigantic. No company has ever grown like that at this scale.

This is the strongest single fact in the whole case. The price already assumes a path nobody has ever walked.

(Quick honesty note: Fortune's own headline flip-flops between "60x" and "600x." The math settles it. $1.1 trillion divided by $18.7 billion is about 59, so the right figure is roughly 60x. I use 60x so nobody can poke a hole in it.)

# Part 4: What is it worth? Even the friendliest number assumes a miracle

I am not the only skeptic, and I am far from the harshest. Two independent valuation shops have put a number on SpaceX. Morningstar pegs fair value at about **$780 billion**, roughly 55% below the price. Aswath Damodaran, the New York University professor known as the "dean of valuation," lands at about **$1.3 trillion**, roughly a quarter below the price. Both say you are overpaying.

People get confused here, because Part 3 says "needs an impossible 60x" and this says "only about a quarter too expensive." Those sound like they fight each other. They do not. They answer two different questions.

Think of a house listed at $1.75 million. Damodaran is the **appraiser**: based on what the business can realistically earn, he says it is worth about $1.3 million, so you are paying about a quarter too much. Trainer is the **rent calculator**: he says if you pay the full $1.75 million asking price and want a normal return, the rent would have to grow to a level no house in this city has ever charged. One says it is worth less than the sticker. The other says even at the sticker you will not make good money without a miracle. Both say walk away.

Now here is where I part ways with Damodaran, and the good news is he publishes his entire model, so I can show you exactly why his $1.3 trillion is the optimistic ceiling, not the floor. Pull up his spreadsheet and look at the sales he assumes each business grows toward by the mid-2030s:

| Business | Revenue Damodaran assumes by ~2036 |
| :-- | :-- |
| Launch | about $40 billion |
| Starlink | about $120 billion |
| **xAI (the AI arm, which swallowed Musk's X, formerly Twitter)** | **about $160 billion** |
| Other moonshots | about $100 billion |

Read that again. His valuation assumes xAI becomes the company's **single largest business by revenue**, $160 billion, ahead of Starlink's $120 billion and four times the launch business, growing from about $3.2 billion in sales today. At its thinner 25% margin it would still earn a bit less profit than Starlink, but by revenue it is the centerpiece of his growth story. And that is the assumption I think is a fantasy. xAI's product, Grok, trails ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, and the unit is burning billions. Damodaran is uneasy about it too: he calls the prospectus's $26 trillion AI market claim something that "borders on fantasy," and his single biggest worry is that SpaceX will "overreach" in AI, warning of "value destruction" if it loses the AI race.

So let me do the arithmetic with his own numbers. His bottom line is a value of operating assets of **$1.224 trillion**, an equity value of **$1.301 trillion**, and a fair value of **$97.83 per share** against the $135 price (his spreadsheet literally flags the price as 1.38 times his value). In his model, xAI throws off about a quarter of the company's mature operating profit (roughly $40 billion of about $160 billion, from his segment revenues and margins). Treat xAI as worth nothing, which is my honest view of a cash-burning also-ran in AI, and as a rough rule of thumb you knock about a quarter off his value, to **roughly $1.0 trillion, about $75 a share.** That is a back-of-envelope haircut, not a rerun of his model, but it shows the direction and the size.

Line up all three reads against the $135 price:

| Estimate | Fair value | Below the $135 price |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Morningstar | about $780 billion | about 55% below |
| Damodaran (as published) | about $1.3 trillion | about 26% below |
| Damodaran minus xAI (my rough haircut) | about $1.0 trillion | about 43% below |

Every serious read says you are overpaying. The cheerful one says by a quarter, and even that rests on xAI becoming the biggest business in SpaceX. Take away that one heroic assumption, which even he is nervous about, and you are overpaying by more like 40% or more. **The best case for buying this stock is "you are only overpaying about 26%." The realistic case is worse.** That is the case.

# Part 5: Where the money actually goes, and a related-party deal worth a hard look

Of the roughly $80 billion SpaceX is raising, only about **$18 billion is uncommitted capital free for new growth.** The other roughly **$62 billion** is already pledged to prior obligations, per research firm New Constructs: about $20 billion to refinance debt SpaceX absorbed from X and xAI, about $22 billion to close the EchoStar spectrum purchase (a real asset that powers Starlink's phone service), and about $20 billion tied to equipment leases with Valor Equity Partners.

That last one deserves a hard look, and these facts come from SpaceX's own filing and from Reuters, not a blog. Valor is the firm of Antonio Gracias, one of Elon Musk's closest friends and a member of SpaceX's board, who through Valor is the second-largest shareholder after Musk (a stake worth roughly $90 billion at this valuation, by press estimates). Starting in late 2025, subsidiaries tied to xAI signed a series of equipment leases with Valor for the chips that run xAI's data centers, obligations of more than $20 billion that SpaceX guarantees. SpaceX had already paid about $885 million under them in 2025, and another $857 million in just the first two months of 2026.

Here is what turns a related-party deal into a red flag. SpaceX's own prospectus accounts for some of these leases as a **"failed sale-leaseback,"** which is accounting language for "this is not really a lease, it is borrowing." In plain terms, the company's own books treat a chunk of this arrangement as debt connected to a director's firm rather than as ordinary rent. Reuters confirmed the filing records billions of dollars of these obligations as debt; one detailed account puts the figure near $9 billion, which I flag as reported rather than independently confirmed.

I am not alleging wrongdoing, and I want to be careful: related-party deals are legal, disclosed, and common, and I have not seen the lease terms. But look at the shape of it. A record-breaking IPO where less than a quarter of the cash funds new growth, where a sitting director's firm sits on the other side of a $20 billion arrangement the company's own accountants will not treat as a normal lease, and where that same director is about to become one of the offering's biggest winners. Even read charitably, that is a tangle the buyer is being asked to fund and trust. Money raised in an IPO is supposed to accelerate the company. A lot of this one settles an interlocking web that mostly traces back to Musk.

# Part 6: The first year is built badly for you

Now look at how the offering is put together, because this is where expensive turns into a setup.

First, the good news, and an honest correction to a common myth: this is an **all-primary offering**. The company is selling brand-new shares and keeping the cash. **No insider sells a single share at the IPO itself.** Nobody is cashing out on day one. If someone tells you Musk is dumping stock on retail at the listing, that is wrong.

The catch is the **lock-up**, the rule for how soon insiders can sell afterward. A normal IPO freezes insiders for 180 days. SpaceX's version lets the early venture investors and employees out far earlier and in stages: about **20%** right after the first quarterly earnings (around August 2026), another **10%** if the stock is trading 30% or more above the IPO price, then **7% chunks** at 70, 90, 105, 120, and 135 days, and another **28%** after the next quarter. Notice the trap in the 30%-above-price clause: a rally itself unlocks more selling, which quietly caps the upside. Meanwhile, the reported plan steers up to **30% of the shares to retail investors** (about $22.5 billion, versus a normal 5% to 10%, though the final number was not locked as of June 9). So a record slice of everyday buyers holds the stock right as the early backers get their early exit.

In fairness again: **Musk himself is locked up for a full 366 days** and gets none of the early releases. So the earliest sellers are the venture funds and staff, not the executives. But governance still tilts hard his way: he controls about **85% of the voting power on roughly 42% of the equity**, with mandatory arbitration and a controlled-company exemption that drew formal objections from the CalPERS and New York pension funds. You are a passenger, not a part-owner with a say.

What does history say happens to hyped IPOs once that supply arrives? Often, a pop and then a long fade. Uber priced at $45 in 2019, closed its first day below that, and sagged for weeks. Rivian priced at $78 in 2021, spiked above $170, and now trades near $16, down about 80%, never recovering. Lyft and Coinbase tell similar stories. I want to be honest, because being sharp means not overclaiming: this is not a law. Uber eventually recovered and trades well above its IPO price today, and Meta fell 54% after its IPO before becoming one of the great winners of the decade. So I am not promising a permanent crash.

My call is narrower and, I think, hard to argue with: **the first six to eighteen months are structurally set up against you.** A nosebleed price with no room for error, a record retail slice, and a staggered wave of insider selling that starts in weeks rather than months. That is a recipe for a pop followed by a supply-heavy grind. Do not chase the opening trade. And if you buy it anyway, call it what it is. It is a gamble, not an investment.

# Part 7: The mistakes even the bears are making

If you are going to argue against this IPO, argue it correctly. Three claims floating around sound damning and are simply wrong. Using them will get your whole case dismissed by anyone who knows the details.

1. **"Index funds will be forced to buy it on day one and dump tens of billions of other stocks."** Mostly false. SpaceX is **blocked from the S&P 500** because it is unprofitable and fails the index's earnings and seasoning rules, and S&P reaffirmed exactly that in June. Some mechanical buying happens later, through the Nasdaq-100 and the FTSE Russell indexes, weeks out, and far smaller than the scare headline. There is no forced day-one fire sale.
2. **"SpaceX could buy Tesla and instantly create hundreds of billions by repricing Tesla's sales at SpaceX's multiple."** That is not how mergers or markets work, and no credible analyst said it. Ignore it.
3. **"At Tesla's nosebleed multiple, SpaceX only needs about $5 billion of profit to justify the price."** The arithmetic technically works, but it quietly assumes a meme-stock multiple is normal (it is not, that is the whole problem), and it ignores that SpaceX is currently losing about $5 billion, not earning it.

I point these out because the real case does not need junk. The price, the growth math, the AI overvaluation, the use of proceeds, and the structure are enough.

# Part 8: The market is not a vacuum. This is the part that matters.

Everything above is the case against SpaceX standing alone. But you never invest in a vacuum. The honest question is never "is SpaceX exciting." It is "where does my next dollar work hardest." And on that question, SpaceX is not even in the conversation.

If you believe the AI story, and I do, then follow it to the places where the math actually adds up rather than the place where it has to be invented. A few directions I am actively working on, and will publish as the analysis firms up:

- **Memory semiconductors.** This is my core thesis. While the crowd chases the glamorous names, memory is the real bottleneck for AI, and it trades at sane multiples with real profits. See [The Memory Play: Why AI's Future Runs on HBM, Not Hype](https://github.com/Veritas-Research/investment-research/blob/main/theses/The%20Memory%20Play%20-%20Why%20AI's%20Future%20Runs%20on%20HBM%2C%20Not%20Hype.md). An updated read follows after the late-June 2026 quarterly print.
- **The power behind AI.** AI does not run on hype, it runs on electricity. Natural gas turbines and Permian Basin natural gas look like a far better-priced way to ride AI's energy demand than a 94-times-sales rocket (a forthcoming Veritas Research note).
- **Home builders.** Real consolidation is underway in the sector, and that usually rewards shareholders. Work in progress.
- **Buyback compounders.** Companies buying back their own shares at reasonable prices are quietly doing the one thing SpaceX cannot: returning value to existing owners.

I am keeping this section directional on purpose. Each of these deserves its own memo with its own math, and I would rather show you the work than wave at tickers. The point here is simpler: the same dollar you would hand to SpaceX at $94 of price for $1 of sales can buy real profits, sane prices, and shareholder returns elsewhere. Held up against those alternatives, the SpaceX bet does not look bold. It looks like a bad trade with a great logo.

---

# Part 9: Where I could be wrong

I hold this view with conviction, so I owe you the other side just as plainly.

"Overpriced" is not the same as "the stock falls tomorrow." Tesla has been called overpriced for a decade and minted millionaires anyway. A great story plus an army of believers can keep a price aloft far longer than the math says it should.

The business is also genuinely excellent. SpaceX dominates global launch and Starlink is growing fast and is profitable. This is not a hollow meme company. The debate is about price, not quality.

And the kinder expert, Damodaran, only sees about a quarter of downside, not a collapse. If xAI surprises me and the AI bet pays off, his number, and the price, could prove low. I think that is unlikely. But I have been wrong about Musk-led companies before, and so has every short seller who underestimated him.

So I am not predicting a crash. I am saying something narrower and, I think, harder to argue with: **you are being asked to pay a price that needs a never-before-seen outcome just to earn an ordinary return, in a deal where most of the money pays old bills, the rosy expert case leans on an AI unit even he is nervous about, and the structure lets early backers leave before you can, when better-priced ways to own the exact same AI story are sitting right there.** That is not the deal of a lifetime. That is the bill of a lifetime. I am passing, and if you understand the facts above and still want in, at least now you are choosing it with your eyes open.
