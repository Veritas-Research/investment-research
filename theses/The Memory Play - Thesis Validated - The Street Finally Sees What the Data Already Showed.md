# Executive Summary (The 2 Minute Version)

**Context:** The whole market is paying for artificial intelligence (AI) the same way: through chips. NVIDIA is, as of early June 2026, the most valuable company on earth. The memory names have run. So have the data center builders, and the companies that sell the power gear. I went looking for the part of the AI buildout the crowd skipped, and I found it somewhere unglamorous: the natural gas molecule that actually runs the data centers.

**What I Found:** The thing holding AI back is no longer chips or buildings. It is power. Nearly 40 percent of data center projects in development are at risk of delay, and Goldman Sachs estimates that only about half of the capacity scheduled for the next two years will arrive on time, with lack of power the leading cause. Nuclear is years away. Wind and solar cannot run a data center around the clock. So natural gas is the only thing that scales inside the window. And US gas is cheap, almost absurdly so: in West Texas this spring, gas at the Waha hub went to a record negative $9.60 per million British thermal units (MMBtu), a glut so bad that producers were paying buyers to take it away.

**What It Means:** Here is the part that took me a while to really get. Almost every way to play this has already been bid up. GE Vernova, the turbine maker, is up about 100 percent in a year. The nuclear utilities, the electrical suppliers, even the old crypto miners that pivoted to AI, all of them have run. The one corner of the chain the market has barely touched is the gas producers. They are cheap. But there is a trap inside that, and getting around it is the whole game.

**The Trap and the Play:** At roughly $3.50 gas, a plain gas producer does not work, because cheap and abundant supply caps the commodity no matter how strong demand gets. The producers that work better are the ones that sold part of their gas straight to data center power plants at a premium over the market price. My highest conviction name is **EQT Corporation (EQT)**, which has signed about 1.4 billion cubic feet per day (Bcf/d) of firm, index plus supply into two Pennsylvania power projects. I want to be precise about what that protects and what it does not: 1.4 Bcf/d is close to one fifth of EQT's roughly 6.5 billion cubic feet equivalent per day (Bcfe/d) of production. It locks a premium on a slice, not on the whole company. My structure: EQT as the highest conviction producer, Cheniere (LNG) as the steady, lowest risk anchor, Expand Energy (EXE) as a deliberate bet on the gas price, and GE Vernova only on a pullback.

**Key Risk:** The biggest risk is not that the thesis is wrong. It is timing. If the AI trade sells off, this whole basket falls with it for a while, no matter how strong gas demand is. This is a bridge, built to work for the next one to four years until nuclear scales toward the end of the decade. Nuclear arriving at scale is the bell that ends it.

# Why I'm Writing This (and How I Built It)

I want to be open about the process, because the process is the point of this series.

I did not start with a stock. I did not even start with my own idea. The spark came from Bob Brackett of Bernstein Research, who argued on The Real Eisman Playbook podcast that the AI power demand story is really a bet on natural gas, and named a few ways to play it. So this is not me uncovering a corner nobody has seen. It is me taking a thesis that is already out there, in public, and stress testing it hard enough to decide whether I actually believe it, and which single version of it survives.

To do that I built a spine, a chain of logic from first principles. AI compute is committed. The buildings need power. Power is the bottleneck. Gas is the only bridge that scales. US gas is cheap. So value should move to whoever owns the gas. Then I went looking for the weak link in that chain, not the links I wanted to be true.

I ran a wide research sweep across demand, the supply chain, valuation, and the bear case, and I put every question through two separate AI research engines so I could see where they agreed (high confidence) and where they disagreed (my job to settle). That cross check caught real mistakes. One source claimed EQT produces 2.1 Bcfe/d when the real number is over 6, a units error that would have wrecked the whole analysis. It caught a subtler one in this very draft too: an early cash flow estimate for Expand Energy leaned on a quarter that a January winter storm had distorted, and only a like for like comparison between the two engines flushed it out. Where a number actually drove a decision, I went to the filing myself. I pulled Expand's quarterly report to confirm its cash flow, and I read EQT's own disclosures to confirm its data center deals.

The result is a thesis where I can point at almost any sentence and tell you where it came from, including the spots where my own research argued against me. I will show you those spots too, because a thesis that buries its own counter evidence is not worth much.

Let's get into it.

# Part 1: The Bottleneck Nobody Priced

The popular AI story is about silicon. I think it is staring at the wrong shortage.

The chips exist. The buildings are going up. What is missing is the electricity to run them. Once you look, the evidence that power is the real constraint is everywhere:

| Signal | Figure | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Data center projects at risk of delay | Nearly 40% | Financial Times / SynMax, April 2026 |
| Scheduled capacity on track to arrive on time | As little as 50% | Goldman Sachs, May 2026 |
| North American data center vacancy | Record low ~1% | JLL, year end 2025 |
| Capacity under construction already committed | ~92% | JLL, year end 2025 |
| PJM grid plant retirements by 2030 vs additions | 40 to 58 GW out, ~31 GW in | Grid filings, 2026 |

That last row is the one that matters most. PJM Interconnection, the largest US grid operator, is losing far more generation to retirements than it is adding, with reserve margins sliding toward 5 percent. This is not a backlog that clears itself. It is a real shortage of firm power that runs to the end of the decade.

One honest caveat on "gas is the only thing that scales inside the window." The turbines that turn gas into electricity are bottlenecked too. The big permanent power plants, the combined cycle gas turbines (CCGTs), are sold out into 2028 to 2030, and even the faster units only show up quickly if a buyer already holds a slot reserved in advance. So gas does not scale freely. What scales inside the next one to four years is power built next to the data center itself, plus whatever slots are already reserved, which rewards the players who already hold gas, sites, and turbine slots. That ceiling is a brake on the bridge, and it is part of why the names with existing positions matter more than the commodity in the abstract.

Here is what told me this was real and not analyst hand waving: follow the money. Inside of a year, the biggest private capital firms on earth stopped buying data centers and started buying power. KKR launched a $10 billion venture, Helix Digital Infrastructure, with NVIDIA, Kuwait's sovereign wealth fund, and the Texas power producer Vistra, built specifically to get around the power bottleneck. Blackstone put more than $25 billion into a gas fired joint venture with the utility PPL. Brookfield committed $5 billion to gas fed fuel cells with Bloom Energy. When KKR, Blackstone, and Brookfield each decide on their own that the way to win the AI buildout is to own the power, that tells me the demand is real.

It also tells me the theme is crowded, and I would rather say that out loud than pretend I found something nobody else can see. The opening that is still there: all that private money is buying the downstream power names, Vistra, PPL, Bloom, and those have already run. It is not buying the upstream gas producers. So the capital validates the demand and still leaves the producers as the part of the chain nobody has bid up. Whether that is an opportunity or a fair price is exactly what Part 3 is about.

And the demand looks durable, which is the single most important answer to the bear case. The old fear is that AI gets more efficient and needs less power. Goldman's work projects token use rising 24 times by 2030 while assuming the cost per token falls 60 to 70 percent a year. In other words, on Goldman's own numbers, even after you price in radical efficiency, demand still goes up, because cheaper compute just gets used more. I lean on this, but I will not oversell it. It is one bank's projection, and it rests on the assumption that usage grows faster than price falls. The near term bear has real data on its side too, which I get into in Part 5. So I treat durable demand as well supported, not settled.

The first claim on the spine holds. Power is the bottleneck, and the shortage is built to last.

# Part 2: The Gold Rush Map (Why Most of the Chain Ran and the Fuel Did Not)

Once you accept that power is the constraint, the obvious move is to buy the power. The problem is that everyone already has.

Picture the AI boom as a gold rush with clear layers. The people selling picks and shovels (chips). The people digging the mines (data centers). The people supplying the gear (turbines, electricals). And the people who own the fuel (gas). When a stock "runs," that is just a polite way of saying the crowd showed up and bid it up before the profits arrived, so you pay full price today for good news everybody already knows. Here is where each layer sits, with prices as of early June 2026:

| Tier | Representative names | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Chips and memory | NVIDIA (~$5T), Broadcom, AMD, Micron | Fully run (Micron the lone cheap one on forward earnings) |
| Builders, operators, electricals | Hyperscalers, CoreWeave, former crypto miners, Vertiv (~54x forward), Eaton | Aggressively run |
| Turbines and nuclear power | GE Vernova (~40 to 45x forward, +100%), Constellation, Talen, Bloom Energy (+1,400%) | Already run |
| **Gas commodity producers** | **EQT (~7.4x forward EV/EBITDA), Expand Energy (down ~18% on the year)** | **Barely touched. The genuinely cheap corner** |

What struck me was not that the power layer is cheap. It is that the power layer is mostly not cheap. The turbine maker is expensive. The nuclear utilities are expensive. Bloom Energy is up roughly 1,400 percent. The wave of private money I described in Part 1 is buying exactly these names. The piece left on the discount rack is the upstream gas producer, the company that owns the molecule everything else runs on. Expand Energy, the largest gas producer in the country, is actually down about 18 percent over the last year, while the gas it sells has never mattered more.

I will give you EQT's valuation with its basis, because "cheap" is a claim you should be able to check: about 7.4 times forward enterprise value to earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization (EV/EBITDA), against a lower number on a trailing basis. It already carries a modest premium versus where it traded a year ago, so "barely touched" is the honest label, not "untouched." The gap is real, but narrower than a clean "nobody owns this" story. The demand is visible and growing, it is fully priced into the turbines and the utilities, and it is only partly priced into the fuel.

# Part 3: The Trap in Plain Sight

This is where most people stop, buy a basket of cheap gas producers, and feel clever. I think that is a trap, and understanding why is the most important part of this whole thesis.

The trap is this. The same cheap gas that powers the demand story also caps the gas producers' stocks.

Look at the price. The latest Energy Information Administration (EIA) outlook puts Henry Hub gas, the US benchmark, at about $3.50 in 2026 and $3.46 in 2027, and the forward curve only reaches about $4.30 by late 2027. The consensus has been drifting down, not up. Why so cheap when demand is booming? Because the country is drowning in gas. In West Texas, gas comes up as a byproduct of oil drilling, and there is so much of it relative to the pipelines that the Waha hub traded at a record negative $9.60 per MMBtu this spring and stayed negative for 47 days straight. Producers out there were paying to give it away.

A clarification I owe you, because it matters for the names later. That negative $9.60 is a West Texas event, driven by Permian pipeline limits. It is the most dramatic picture of how much gas this country has, but it is not the price the producers in this thesis actually get. EQT sells Appalachian gas priced off the Marcellus, which is cheap but never sees Waha's negative prints. I use Waha to make one point only: supply is so heavy that it caps the commodity across the country. I am not suggesting EQT sells gas at negative prices.

For a normal producer, that cap is a problem you cannot escape. If you just pump gas and sell it at the market price, then no matter how much AI demand grows, your stock stays cheap as long as the commodity stays cheap. Being right about demand does not put a dollar in your pocket.

So the question that actually matters is not "is gas demand going up." It is "who gets paid more than the market price, and on how much of their gas." That narrows the field fast, and it is what Part 4 is about.

# Part 4: The Way Through

There are only a few ways to own this thesis that survive a $3.50 gas price. They are the players who lock in a price above the market, or earn a fixed fee no matter what gas does, or make money on purpose when gas rises.

## The Highest Conviction Producer: EQT Corporation (EQT)

EQT did the one thing that softens the trap. Instead of selling all of its gas into the open market at the cheap price, it signed long term contracts to supply gas straight to data center power plants at an index plus price, meaning the local market rate plus a premium. Across two Pennsylvania projects, Homer City and Shippingport, it has locked in about 1.4 Bcf/d of firm supply starting in 2027. I checked this against EQT's own disclosures and trade reporting: the contracts are signed and priced on a Marcellus index plus basis, what the desks call M2 plus.

I want to be careful about what index plus does, because it is easy to oversell. It is not a fixed floor. It is the local index price plus a premium, so EQT still cares where gas trades, it just always earns a little more than that. The honest analogy is a dairy farmer who sells 20 of his 100 gallons a day on a "market plus fifty cents" deal. He still feels it when milk gets cheap, because 80 gallons sell at the going rate, and even the protected 20 are priced off that same going rate. The fifty cents guarantees he beats the local price on that slice. It does not make him immune to the price. EQT is that farmer. The 1.4 Bcf/d under contract is close to one fifth of its roughly 6.5 Bcfe/d of production. The other four fifths is ordinary gas sold at the market price, and EQT barely hedges it (about 15 to 16 percent of 2026 volume, at a floor near $3.57).

So the right way to see EQT is a low cost producer with a protected corner, which still leaves most of the company riding the gas price. One note on sourcing: the headline 1.4 Bcf/d and the index plus structure are solidly corroborated, but the exact size of the premium rests on a single source, so treat the size of the spread as directional, not nailed down. What makes EQT my highest conviction producer is the combination. A protected slice. The lowest cash cost in its basin, roughly $1.09 to $1.30 per thousand cubic feet equivalent. And a stock still at a single digit forward multiple. It is the producer I would most want to own. It is not the lowest risk name in the stack. That title goes to the next one.

## The Steady Anchor: Cheniere Energy (LNG)

Cheniere turns US gas into liquid and ships it overseas, and it gets paid a fixed fee under contracts of 15 to 20 years that cover about 95 percent of its capacity. Think of it as a toll booth. It collects the toll whether gas is cheap or expensive. On its contracted volumes it is insulated from the gas price rather than a bet on it, with a little extra upside on its own spot volumes. It trades at roughly an 8 percent free cash flow yield, well above its midstream peers. This is the lowest volatility way to own the theme, and the closest thing here to a sleep at night holding. Worth saying plainly: Cheniere is less an AI bet than a bet that the world wants cheap, reliable US gas, and that is a feature. It does not need the AI story to go perfectly, and global demand for energy security only reinforces it.

## The Torque Sleeve: Expand Energy (EXE)

Expand is the deliberate opposite of EQT. It is the largest US gas producer, it does not have the premium direct supply contracts, and it barely hedges, so it is close to a pure bet on the gas price itself. I am not going to pretend the market has this one wrong. A producer that barely hedges should be cheap when the forward curve says gas stays soft, and that is exactly why Expand is down about 18 percent on the year. I would own it, in small and deliberate size, only as an explicit bet that gas firms up above the consensus curve.

The math shows why it is a sleeve and not a core holding. I pulled its quarterly filing and modeled both ends. At $5 gas it throws off roughly $20 per share of free cash flow against a stock around $90, a spectacular yield. At the $3.50 base case it throws off only about $4 to $6 per share, an ordinary yield in the mid single digits. Same wells, same company... the entire case is a bet on the gas price rising. (That $4 to $6 estimate holds the price discount steady across both scenarios, the only assumption that is consistent with the $20 figure at $5 gas that the filing confirms. An early draft used a discount distorted by winter weather and overstated it, which the two engine cross check caught.) If your read on gas is no better than the curve, you should not own this at all.

## The Bottleneck Winner, on Weakness Only: GE Vernova (GEV)

GE Vernova is one of only three companies on earth that make large gas turbines, and its order book is sold out into 2028 to 2030 with a $163 billion backlog. It is a wonderful business. It is also up about 100 percent in a year and trades at 40 to 45 times forward earnings. The demand is already in the price. I would only add it on a real pullback, not here.

Here is the stack in one view:

| Name | Ticker | Role | Why it works (or does not) at $3.50 gas | Valuation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| EQT | EQT | Highest conviction producer | 1.4 Bcf/d signed index plus (~1/5 of output); lowest cost; the rest still rides the price | ~$33B cap, ~7.4x forward EV/EBITDA |
| Cheniere | LNG | Steady anchor (lowest risk) | Fixed fee, ~95% contracted; insulated from the commodity | ~8% free cash flow yield |
| Expand Energy | EXE | Torque sleeve (bull case only) | It does not; ~$4 to $6/share FCF at $3.50, ~$20 at $5 | ~$22B cap, no AI premium |
| GE Vernova | GEV | Turbine (on a pullback) | Decoupled from the commodity, but already expensive | ~40 to 45x forward, +100% |

EV/EBITDA is enterprise value to earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization.

One name sits on the watch list, not in the stack. **Comstock Resources (CRK)** has announced the single biggest potential deal in the group, a 5.2 gigawatt gas hub in Texas that NextEra would build to serve data centers, fed by up to 1 Bcf/d of Comstock gas. I checked it against Comstock's own release and the size is real, but the same release says it is "subject to negotiation and execution of definitive documents," so it is a selection, not a signed contract. The gas does not flow until 2031, and Comstock carries more debt. A 2031 start sits past the back end of this bridge, so I am keeping it on the watch list rather than in the position. If the definitive documents get signed, it becomes a live, high torque option.

# Part 5: What Would Make Me Wrong

I size this thesis the way I do because I can see how it loses. There are two ways, on two different clocks.

**The near term risk is timing, not the thesis.** The real danger is not that AI stops needing power. It is that the AI trade sells off on a sentiment or efficiency scare, and the whole basket, the cheap gas names included, goes down together because the same momentum funds own all of it. In a risk off move these names fall together no matter how their individual fundamentals look, so spreading across the chain does not save you on the day. And the bear is not hypothetical. In the past year Microsoft cancelled roughly 200 megawatts of leases, Meta cancelled about $4 billion of US leases, Amazon paused some leases, and Microsoft's own chief executive said out loud, "there will be an overbuild." That is real evidence that some of the announced demand is softening at the edges, and it is the strongest card the near term bear holds. The trigger I watch is a cut to hyperscaler capital spending guidance of more than 15 percent, or two quarters of cuts in a row, earliest in the back half of 2026. This is a "right thesis, wrong entry price" risk, and the answer is patience on the entry, not avoidance.

**The medium term risk is that cheap gas stays cheap.** If Permian supply and a coming wave of liquefied natural gas (LNG) export capacity keep Henry Hub capped, the producers without direct contracts go nowhere even as their volumes grow. This is exactly why EQT, with its protected slice and its low cost, sits above Expand, which needs the price to cooperate.

My rough probability weights over three years: bull 42 percent, base 39 percent, bear 19 percent. I want to be careful here, because my own validation work cut both ways. The demand side held up strongly, which makes me more confident in the theme. But the same work showed the price taking producers are riskier than I first thought. Gas in 2027 looks moderate, not rising. EQT barely hedges most of its book. The LNG glut and the overbuild risk are both live. So the honest net is not "more bullish on everything." It is more confident in the theme, and more selective inside it, leaning toward contracted and decoupled exposure (Cheniere, GE Vernova on weakness, and EQT's protected slice) over the pure price takers. Past 2030 the bear case roughly doubles as nuclear and other options finally arrive, which is the whole reason I treat this as a bridge with a shelf life, not a forever hold.

| Risk | Probability | What it does | What to watch |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI capital spending pullback | Medium | Whole basket sells off on sentiment, multiples compress | A cut to hyperscaler spending guidance over 15%, or two quarters of cuts; more lease cancellations |
| Gas stays cheap | Medium | Price takers go nowhere; favors EQT and Cheniere over Expand | Henry Hub failing to hold the low $3s; an LNG glut |
| Nuclear arrives early | Low before 2029 | Removes the reason for the bridge | Small modular reactor dates; the bell that ends it |

The honest version of my conviction: high on the spine, high on the sub sector, high on EQT as the best producer to own, highest of all on Cheniere as the safest way in, and least sure about timing the entry. That is why this reads as a framework, not a "buy it now" alarm.

# What Comes Next

Three things I am watching between now and the next update:

1. **The next round of hyperscaler capital spending guidance (third and fourth quarter 2026).** This is the single most important near term tell. If the guidance holds, the selloff risk stays low and the pull on gas demand stays intact. If it cracks, or if the lease cancellations spread, the whole complex sells off first and asks questions later.

2. **EQT's direct supply contracts turning into cash (2027).** Homer City and Shippingport are scheduled to start in 2027. Watching those volumes actually flow at the contracted premium is what turns the EQT thesis from a story into a number.

3. **The nuclear clock.** Constellation's restart of Three Mile Island to serve Microsoft is targeted for 2027, the leading edge of the nuclear wave. One restart is far too small to matter against 50 to 65 gigawatts of gas demand, so it does not end the bridge. The signal to start trimming is when credible commercial nuclear for data centers scales toward the 2029 to 2030 window, not the first single site restart. When that clock moves from "around 2030" toward "now," I start cutting.

I said this series is about showing the work, not just the call. So here is the call, plainly. The crowd bought the picks, the shovels, and the turbines, and left the fuel sitting there. Most of the fuel is a trap, because cheap gas caps the price takers. The way through is the part of the chain that beats the commodity: EQT's contracted slice plus its low cost, Cheniere's fixed fee toll booth, and GE Vernova on weakness, with Expand as a small, deliberate bet on gas rising. It is the corner of the AI buildout the market has touched the least, owned with discipline instead of as one clever ticker. I will update this in public as the contracts convert and the spending numbers land.
