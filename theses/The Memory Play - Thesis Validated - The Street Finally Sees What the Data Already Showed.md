The timing of these results with NVIDIA's GTC 2026 (March 15-18) was not coincidental. Here's what Micron confirmed:

## The Post-Earnings Selloff: Why It Happened and Why I'm Not Worried

Despite all of this, Micron stock fell roughly 3.8% the day of earnings to $442 and has since pulled back further to $355, down about 24.6% from its 52-week high of $471.

Three factors explain the disconnect:

1. **Elevated capex.** Micron guided to $25B+ in FY2026 capex, up 80% year-over-year. That creates near-term free cash flow drag even as revenue explodes.
2. **Broader AI sector compression.** The entire AI trade has been under pressure from profit-taking after 550%+ gains since April 2025. This isn't Micron-specific.
3. **Tariff uncertainty.** The April 14 USTR/Commerce report is hanging over every semiconductor name. Until that clears, the market won't pay full freight.

Here's what I think the market is getting wrong: it's applying peak-cycle skepticism to a business whose structural demand profile has fundamentally changed. Five-year supply agreements, HBM4 in volume production, 81% guided gross margins. This isn't the old memory cycle where you made hay for 18 months and then watched prices collapse for three years. The supply constraints are structural, not cyclical, and the packaging bottleneck alone ensures normalization is a 2028+ phenomenon at the earliest.

I'm treating the pullback as the market giving me a better entry point, not as a signal to reduce.

# Part 4: Thesis Checkpoints

In Part 1, I identified specific risks and monitoring triggers. Here's where each one stands two months later.

## HBM Supply: Still Sold Out Through 2027

**Status: Fully intact.**

SK Hynix confirmed at its Q3 2025 earnings (October 29, 2025) that 2026 HBM supply was fully allocated and supply would remain "slightly short of demand even in 2027." Their exact words: "We have been sold out since 2023, and prices have formed at a level where profitability can be maintained."

To meet demand, SK Hynix announced in January 2026 a 19 trillion KRW ($12.9B) investment in a new packaging facility (P&T7) in Cheongju, with construction starting April 2026 and completion targeted end of 2027. A second facility (M15X) with 20 trillion KRW committed is on track for full utilization by mid-2027, producing approximately 50,000 wafers per month.

This is bullish, not bearish. You don't commit $12.9 billion to a new facility unless your order book extends well beyond the current horizon. SK Hynix is building for demand they've already contracted.

The critical constraint remains packaging and TSV stacking, not wafer capacity. Through-silicon via assembly and advanced bumping require specialized cleanrooms with 2-3 year lead times. Even with aggressive capex, supply normalization is a 2028+ phenomenon at the earliest. This was one of the core structural arguments in Part 1, and it's only gotten stronger.

## Samsung's HBM Qualification: Closing the Gap, Not Closing It

**Status: Partially materializing, as expected.**

This was the top risk I flagged in January, and the picture is now clearer:

- **HBM3E (12-Hi):** Samsung reportedly cleared NVIDIA's qualification following a redesign to address heat and yield issues (September 2025 TrendForce report). However, Samsung's HBM market share remained depressed at approximately 17% in H1 2025, versus SK Hynix at 62% and Micron at 21%.
- **HBM4:** Both SK Hynix and Samsung delivered paid HBM4 samples to NVIDIA as of December 2025, moving beyond free prototypes into pre-contract commercial testing. Samsung's HBM4 reportedly cleared NVIDIA's required tests (August 2025 reports), with plans for mass production.
- **Current position:** Samsung is likely to become the second-largest HBM4 supplier after SK Hynix, per SeDaily. Samsung is also reportedly supplying HBM to Broadcom with positive feedback.

**My assessment:** Samsung re-entry into NVIDIA's supply chain is happening, but it's additive, not replaceable. Vera Rubin's architecture requires vastly more HBM per unit than Hopper or Blackwell, meaning even at a three-way supply split, absolute revenue per supplier grows. SK Hynix's leadership (JEITA-standard in-house foundry plus mature yield advantage) is unlikely to be meaningfully closed before the HBM5 generation.

The January thesis assumed Samsung would make progress. It's playing out roughly as modeled. This risk downgrades from "high uncertainty" to "known factor, manageable."

## Tariff Risk: April 14, the Only Date That Matters

**Status: Elevated. This is the single biggest variable.**

The April 14 deadline is now the most important near-term event for both positions. Here's the full picture:

**Phase 1 (January 15, 2026):** Trump signed a proclamation imposing a 25% tariff on a narrow category of advanced computing chips (primarily Nvidia H200, AMD MI325X). Memory chips were explicitly excluded from Phase 1.

**Phase 2 (April 14 report):** The USTR and Commerce Department must deliver their report on broader semiconductor tariffs after Phase 2 negotiations conclude. Commerce Secretary Lutnick has warned non-US-building chipmakers face "likely 100%" tariffs.

**Current Korea-US trade status:** A 15% tariff rate under the July 2025 US-Korea deal remains in effect with chips exempt under ongoing negotiations. A 25% tariff on "selected chips" remains in place but has not been applied to memory.

**SK Hynix's US exposure:** The company has committed approximately $4.1B in US investment (Indiana HBM packaging plant), versus Samsung's $38.9B US commitment. Neither matches TSMC's $165B precedent, and Washington's benchmark appears to be dedicated US DRAM fabrication.

**USTR Section 301 investigation:** On March 10, USTR initiated Section 301 investigations against 16 trade partners, with an unofficial July 24, 2026 target date for remedy determinations.

**My assessment:** Phase 2 tariffs on memory chips remain the most severe risk to the thesis. A 25% blanket tariff on Korean memory imports would pressure SK Hynix margins by an estimated 8-12 percentage points annually and force supply chain restructuring. However, the political calculus is complicated: US data centers, NVIDIA's primary customers, rely entirely on Korean HBM, so tariffs would hurt US hyperscalers more than Korean suppliers in the near term. South Korea's Trade Minister noted the "limited immediate impact" precisely because the US has no domestic HBM alternative.

My tariff probability distribution from January was:

| Tariff Level | January Probability | March Update |
|---|---|---|
| None (0-5%) | 10% | 10% (unchanged) |
| Symbolic (10-15%) | 60% | 55% (slightly reduced) |
| Moderate (25-30%) | 20% | 25% (slightly increased) |
| Aggressive (50%+) | 10% | 10% (unchanged) |

I've shifted 5% from "symbolic" to "moderate" based on the Section 301 investigation and Lutnick's increasingly aggressive rhetoric. The base case is still a negotiated outcome, but the tail risk is marginally higher.

## AMI Labs and Neuro-Symbolic AI: Still a Watch Item, Not a Threat

**Status: Monitor only. Not a 12-month catalyst.**

In Part 1, I discussed Yann LeCun's world models thesis as one of the reasons memory wins across all AI paradigms. Since then, AMI Labs raised $1.03B at a $3.5B valuation in what's being described as the largest seed round ever by a European startup. They're building world models using JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture), predicting in compressed latent spaces rather than token streams.

Why this matters for the HBM thesis:

- AMI's target domains (robotics, embodied AI, industrial process control) inherently require persistent state memory and real-time spatial processing, not just transformer-style KV caches
- If AMI-style architectures gain traction over 3-5 years, the type of memory demanded shifts: less pure KV-cache HBM, more persistent working memory with tight latency constraints. Arguably still favorable for HBM given its bandwidth profile.
- AMI CEO LeBrun acknowledged the company won't have commercial products for multiple years: "It's not your typical applied AI startup that can release a product in three months."
- LeCun's JEPA architecture is compute-efficient by design, which could reduce per-inference memory bandwidth requirements. A long-run risk to raw HBM demand assumptions.

**My verdict:** AMI Labs validates the "memory wins all paradigms" framework from Part 1, not threatens it. Even LeCun's alternative to transformers needs high-bandwidth memory. The near-term HBM supercycle remains LLM/inference-driven, and AMI is years from production. This does not change the thesis over the investment horizon.

**SK Hynix:** Trading at a 15% discount to its 52-week high despite selling out 2026 production and confirming 2027 shortage conditions. The 19% CAGR assumption now appears conservative for the first 2-3 years given the 38-analyst consensus average target implies roughly 38% upside from current levels, and the company is ramping a $12.9B packaging facility to serve orders already contracted. A revised base case 5-year CAGR of **20-25%** is reasonable.

**The condition:** These revised projections are conditional on the April 14 USTR report not imposing aggressive (25%+) tariffs on memory chips. If Phase 2 tariffs come in at the moderate or aggressive end of my probability distribution, the SK Hynix CAGR compresses to 12-15% and the allocation needs to shift toward Micron. I will formally lock in revised numbers after the April 14 report provides clarity.

## Allocation Verdict

The **60/40 SK Hynix / Micron split remains structurally sound.** The Q2 FY2026 blowout and Q3 guidance argue for maintaining full position weight rather than trimming into the post-earnings pullback.

The stock market reaction, Micron down roughly 24% from its all-time high despite record earnings and guidance, is characteristic of peak-cycle recognition anxiety. The market remembers that memory stocks have peaked before. It's applying a "this time isn't different" discount. I think this time is at least partially different because of five-year supply agreements, packaging bottlenecks with 2-3 year lead times, and HBM's structural role in AI infrastructure. But I also acknowledge I could be wrong, which is why the position is sized at 5% of portfolio and not 15%.
