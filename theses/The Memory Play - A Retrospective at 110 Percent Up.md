The March memo flagged Micron's first SCA as a "potentially transformative disclosure." I underweighted how fast the rest of the industry would follow. This was the cleanest unforeseen positive.

## 2.1 The Vera Rubin assumption taken at face value

The single biggest process error of the March memo was accepting Micron's Graphics Technology Conference (GTC) 2026 press release language at face value. Micron's release described HBM4 36GB 12-High as "designed for NVIDIA's Vera Rubin GPU platform" and entering "high-volume production" in Q1 calendar 2026. I wrote the March memo treating Micron's HBM4 qualification and Vera Rubin allocation as effectively synonymous.

They are not synonymous. SemiAnalysis cut Micron's Vera Rubin allocation to approximately 0% in February 2026 after Micron failed NVIDIA's 11 Gigabits per second (Gbps) specification. The initial Vera Rubin HBM4 production allocation went to SK Hynix at approximately 70% and Samsung at approximately 30%. NVIDIA is employing a dual-binning strategy with Samsung providing high-spec chips at approximately $700 per unit and SK Hynix supplying volume at $600 to $700.

The methodological error is the one I want to name. I cited a company press release as if it were independent technical sourcing. SemiAnalysis had the contradicting data published in February. I did not check. That is a gap in the research process, and the gap is on me, not on the data.

I should also be honest about the catch-up framing. Micron's entire 2026 HBM4 production is sold out to non-NVIDIA customers (likely including Marvell-led custom Application-Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs), Advanced Micro Devices (AMD)-adjacent through Marvell, Amazon Web Services (AWS), and Meta). That is a real revenue floor for 2026. The 12-month catch-up window to a potential Vera Rubin Ultra qualification in H2 2027 is the bullish recovery framing the brief and the Micron press release imply. I want to flag that the 12-month catch-up is an assumption, not a confirmed NVIDIA roadmap. If HBM4E qualification slips into FY2028, the optical loss extends. I do not have independent confirmation of the H2 2027 timeline beyond Micron's own commentary and analyst inference.

## 2.2 April 14 anchored as a binary trigger

In March, I wrote that the April 14 USTR Phase 2 report was "the single biggest variable" and "the only date that matters." I held conviction at 80% specifically conditional on that outcome.

April 14 was an internal Commerce Department and USTR reporting milestone under Proclamation 11002, signed January 14, 2026. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and USTR Greer delivered a private 90-day status update to the President. No tariff proclamation was issued. No public action was taken. Memory chips remain explicitly excluded from Phase 1 tariffs. The Micron stock move of +9.1% on April 14 was driven primarily by ASML's Q1 beat and full-year guidance raise to €36 to €40 billion, plus Arete Research raising its target to $852. Not by the tariff outcome itself, because there was no tariff outcome.

The methodological error is anchoring conviction on a single binary date. The single-binary-date conviction framework is fragile in two directions. If the date turns out to be procedural (as April 14 did), the conditional resolves favorably but for the wrong reason. If the date had carried a substantive ruling, the framework would have collapsed conviction into a yes/no on a single proclamation. Either outcome is a poor representation of the actual risk structure.

The post-February 20 Supreme Court ruling on International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) reciprocal tariffs reshaped the trade architecture in a way the March memo could not have anticipated. The Court invalidated IEEPA-based 15% tariffs on Korea; the administration replaced them with a 10% global Section 122 surcharge, with semiconductors exempt. That ruling moved the relevant deadlines from one date (April 14) to a rolling set (July 1, July 24, August Section 301 conclusions, November 10, June 23, 2027). The framework now has to handle distributed risk, not point risk.

I am taking the methodological lesson explicitly: future memos will de-emphasize binary triggers in favor of rolling probability assessments. I will revisit this in Section 4 when I describe how the conviction framework changes.

## 2.3 Samsung pace under-modeled

The March memo described Samsung as "closing the gap, not closing it" and downgraded Samsung competition risk from "high uncertainty" to "known factor, manageable."

That description is no longer accurate. Samsung now holds approximately 30% of the NVIDIA Vera Rubin allocation. Q1 2026 operating profit was ₩57.2 trillion (+755% YoY), an all-time record. Samsung's Pyeongtaek 4-nanometer logic foundry is over 50% allocated captive to HBM4 base dies. Samsung won an HBM4 exclusive supply agreement with OpenAI for the Titan Generation 1 chip (designed by Broadcom, fabbed at Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) in Q3 2026), representing approximately 7% of Samsung's 2026 HBM4 output.

The foundry-HBM4 vertical integration is the structural advantage I most under-modeled. Micron does not own logic foundry capacity at advanced nodes. SK Hynix relies on TSMC for HBM4 base dies. Samsung produces them in-house at Pyeongtaek. The "tier-one HBM is a three-way oligopoly" framing remains correct. The competitive dynamics within that oligopoly are not symmetric, and Samsung's integration is a structural feature, not a yield contest.

Yield is the caveat. Samsung HBM4 yields sit at approximately 60% (TrendForce April 2026 yield bulletin) against an 85% year-end 2026 target (SamMobile, March 2026). The gap between current and target yield is real and is not closing as fast as Samsung's own roadmap. That keeps the competition rating at "material but not yet catastrophic" rather than "structurally dominant."

I want to be clear about the re-rating. In March I had Samsung at "manageable." In May I have Samsung at "material but not yet catastrophic." That is a one-tier upgrade in the risk register. It is not a thesis-breaker for Micron, because Micron's 2026 revenue does not depend on NVIDIA share specifically. But it is a meaningfully higher bar for FY2027 and FY2028 share recovery.

## 2.5 The single-binary-conviction framework as a methodological issue

Beyond April 14 specifically, the deeper error is the framework itself. In March I expressed conviction as "80% conditional on the April 14 outcome." That is one number, one date, and a conditional that resolves in a single binary. When the date passed without action, the conditional resolved favorably, but the conviction number did not have a clean way to update for everything else that had changed (the Vera Rubin reallocation, Samsung's pace, the LTA shift, the capex revisions, the legal architecture change).

The fix for Section 4 of this memo is to retire single-binary-conviction framing. Conviction in v2 is reported as two separate numbers: structural thesis confidence, and risk-adjusted position confidence at the current price. The two are different and they should be reported separately. I will return to this.

## 3.8 Bear arguments steelmanned

I want to engage each of these in their strongest form, not as strawmen. The structural argument I am making does not require the bears to be wrong about everything. It requires me to identify what specifically distinguishes 2026 from prior peaks, and to be honest where the distinction is structural and where it is a hopeful read.

### Records-simultaneity as cycle peak signature

The bear read: every prior memory cycle peak featured all-time records at every supplier simultaneously. SK Hynix Q1 72% operating margin (record), Samsung Q1 +755% YoY operating profit (record), Micron Q2 FY2026 beat of 31% on EPS. The pattern matches 2018 and 2022 peaks. The question is what specifically distinguishes 2026 from prior peaks.

The structural argument I would make: LTAs covering large fractions of hyperscaler volume (estimated approximately 60 to 70% of HBM commercial output by end of 2026, though I cannot confirm a precise industry-wide number); the packaging bottleneck with 2 to 3 year lead times that prior cycles did not have; and the HBM oligopoly structure (three suppliers, capacity constrained, integrated buyer relationships) versus the broader DRAM commodity market in 2018 and 2022.

The honest read: those are structural differences. They are not a definitive claim that this is not the peak. The bear read on records-simultaneity is correct that pattern recognition would call this a peak signature. The structural read is that this peak signature could prove more durable than prior ones because of the LTA and oligopoly structure. I will not overclaim by saying "this is not the peak." I will say "the peak signature could prove more durable because of identifiable structural differences."

### Capex spike as a 4-to-6-quarter-ahead peak indicator

The bear read: Samsung 2026 capex +22% YoY, SK Hynix +33%, Micron +80%. Historically, memory capex peaks 4 to 6 quarters before revenue peaks. The 2018 and 2022 peaks fit this pattern. By that pattern, the +80% Micron capex spike in early 2026 implies a revenue peak in H1 2027.

The structural counter: the current capex spike is concentrated in HBM packaging, advanced bumping, and process technology rather than greenfield cleanroom expansion. Combined Applied Materials, Lam Research, Tokyo Electron Q1 commentary distinguished between equipment for HBM/packaging (heavy book-to-bill) and greenfield DRAM cleanroom (lighter). That mix shift matters because HBM and process capex flows into the supply-constrained portions of the stack, while greenfield cleanroom is the leading indicator of commodity oversupply.

The honest read: the lead-lag pattern is a real bear point. If the capex mix continues to shift toward greenfield cleanroom over the next 2 to 4 quarters, the bear pattern becomes much more concerning. Signal 3 in the trim framework (Section 4) captures this directly.

### The 2027 to 2028 supply wall

The bear read: new memory capacity coming online in 2027 to 2028 represents the most coordinated capacity add of any prior cycle. Samsung Taylor (Texas), SK Hynix Yongin (Korea) and Indiana (United States), Micron Idaho One and Tongluo (Taiwan, via the Powerchip Taipei acquisition). The cumulative wafer additions are the largest the industry has ever seen converging into a single 18-month window.

I do not have precise per-fab wafers-per-month figures that I trust enough to publish here. The brief flagged the fabs by name and timing but did not provide per-fab capacity additions in a form I would cite. What I can say is that this is the largest coordinated capacity add the memory industry has seen, that the LTA structure absorbs a meaningful but undefined portion of it through pre-committed volume, and that the non-hyperscaler segments (smartphone, Personal Computer (PC), automotive) remain largely uncontracted and would absorb the residual.

The honest read: the bear has the more specific argument here. The structural read relies on LTA absorption that I cannot quantify with precision. If anyone asks me what percentage of 2027 to 2028 industry output will be under LTAs by the time the new capacity ramps, I do not have a confident answer. That is a real gap.

### DDR5 and HBM3E margin convergence direction

The bear read: HBM3E margins have fallen to converge with DDR5 margins (Samsung HBM3E discount in Q4 2025), implying pricing power dissipation at the high end. The bull read: DDR5 margins have risen to meet HBM3E (server DDR5 RDIMM doubling in Q1 2026), implying broad-based pricing power.

The honest read: both have happened. Samsung discounted HBM3E in Q4 2025 to compete, then took a +20% price hike in 2026. DDR5 doubled. The convergence is now from both sides. The dominant direction since Q4 2025 has been DDR5 rising to HBM3E (in profitability terms), with the brief flagging that server DDR5 RDIMM contract profitability has overtaken HBM3E by Q2 2026. That is the more bullish read. But the convergence does mean HBM3E's high-margin moat is narrowing, not widening. I want to be honest about that.

### China memory as a 2027 risk, not 2028+

The bear read: ChangXin Memory Technologies (CXMT) targets HBM3 8-high mass production in 2026 and 12-high HBM3 and HBM3E in 2027. CXMT filed for an Initial Public Offering (IPO) on the Shanghai STAR Market targeting $2.8 to $5.6 billion. Yangtze Memory Technologies (YMTC) holds approximately 12% global NAND share with three new Wuhan fabs each targeting approximately 100,000 wafers per month.

In March I treated China memory as a 2028+ supply headwind. The CXMT HBM3 timeline (2026 mass production, 2027 12-high HBM3 and HBM3E) is a 2027 inflection, not a 2028+ one. I am re-rating this risk from "2028+ supply headwind that lengthens the runway" to "2027 inflection that compresses the back end of the runway." For the 12 to 18 month target horizon, this still does not invalidate the thesis, but the runway is shorter than I previously argued.

### Customer concentration: Apple's exit signal

Apple is paying approximately $1 billion per year to Google for Gemini AI access, signaling that Apple has functionally exited the AI infrastructure arms race. AI infrastructure capex is concentrating in four to five hyperscalers (Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta, Amazon, Oracle, with CoreWeave as a leveraged sixth). That is a real concentration risk for memory demand.

If two of the top five hyperscalers materially reduce 2027 capex guidance, the memory demand thesis compresses substantially. The bear read is correct that demand has narrowed in concentration even as it has expanded in absolute size. The structural read is that contracted LTA volume mitigates the concentration risk for memory specifically, but not entirely.

### Forward 5x P/E framing is conditional

I already addressed this in Section 3.2. The 5.2x forward multiple on $747 holds only on the bull EPS of $143. On the bear EPS of $55, the same $747 price implies a forward multiple of approximately 13.6x. The "below historical peak multiples" framing is circular. The cheap multiple requires the high EPS. Saying "Micron trades at 5x forward" without the EPS conditional is misleading. I want to be explicit about that.

### LTAs as buyer hedges renegotiated when buyer economics turn

The bear read: LTAs are buyer hedges. They are renegotiated when buyer economics change. In prior memory downturns, when end-market demand collapsed and hyperscaler capex was cut, LTAs were systematically renegotiated to remove upside-only pricing structures. The current LTA shift assumes the buyers maintain their current economics indefinitely, which is not the historical pattern.

The honest read: this is a legitimate bear point with no clean historical analog. The current LTAs are structured differently from prior LTAs (volume plus price floors, multi-year terms, prepayments). But the underlying logic is the same: the buyer is hedging against shortage. If the shortage turns to surplus, the hedge becomes a contractual obligation the buyer wants to escape. There is no historical case where multi-year price-floor memory LTAs have been tested through a real downturn at the scale currently being signed. I am flagging this as an open question.

### Goldman's capex and Free Cash Flow (FCF) concern

Goldman's $400 Neutral has been correct that Micron capex would escalate. FY2026 capex is now above $25 billion (raised from $20 billion guidance in March). Q3 capex was raised to approximately $7 billion. Goldman's bear thesis is that high capex compresses FCF at the exact moment when peak-cycle revenues are most vulnerable, and that the multiple compresses defensively as a result, regardless of reported EPS.

I want to flag this as an open data point in this memo. I do not have explicit FY2027 to FY2028 FCF estimates that I trust enough to publish. The consolidated brief did not contain them. The Goldman concern is structurally sound and points at a real risk: even if EPS hits $143 in FY2027, if FCF goes flat or negative against $25 billion+ capex, the equity multiple compresses regardless. I would rather flag this gap honestly than produce a fabricated FCF trajectory. If a reader has the modeling for it, I would welcome the correction in feedback. The next follow-up memo (post-Q3 FY2026 print) should fill this gap with management commentary on capital intensity through FY2028.

## 3.9 The AI capex bubble, engaged seriously

This deserves its own sub-section, not a paragraph. Three frameworks are circulating in institutional discourse:

**Sequoia Capital's David Cahn $125 billion "hole" framework.** Cahn's argument is that the aggregate annual revenue required to justify cumulative AI capex grows by approximately $125 billion per year at current spending pace. To justify $725 billion in 2026 hyperscaler capex with a 15% Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) threshold, the implied AI revenue requirement is approximately $109 billion annualized for that year's incremental capital alone. Cumulative through 2027 to 2028, the figure becomes large enough that AI revenue would need to compound at extreme rates to clear ROIC hurdles.

**JPMorgan ROIC framework.** JPMorgan has published an ROIC framework arguing that current hyperscaler AI infrastructure spending may not generate adequate returns at the spending trajectory implied by 2026 to 2027 guidance. The framework does not produce a single number but does flag capex/revenue ratios at "previously unthinkable" levels: Oracle approximately 86%, Meta approximately 54%, Microsoft approximately 47%, Alphabet approximately 46%.

**Capex sustainability conditional.** The bull case for memory requires hyperscaler capex to be sustained or grow into 2027 and 2028. If two of the top five hyperscalers materially trim 2027 capex guidance (or even just stop revising upward), the memory demand thesis compresses. The trim framework in Section 4 includes peer gross margin and FCF plateau as one of the cycle peak signals precisely because hyperscaler capex deceleration would show up there first.

I am stating my conviction on AI capex sustainability separately from my conviction on the memory thesis. On the memory structural thesis, my confidence is high. On AI capex sustainability through 2027 to 2028, my confidence is meaningfully lower. The two are linked but not identical, and they should be reported separately.

# Section 4: What We Suggest (and Why)

## 4.1 The recommendation in one paragraph

For existing holders: maintain the existing position with the trim ladder active starting at $850. For new capital deploying at current price: size at 2 to 3% of portfolio rather than the original 5%, because the upside-to-downside skew has collapsed from approximately 11-to-1 in March to approximately 1.1-to-1 today. Re-evaluate the framework after the Q3 FY2026 print on June 24 or 25. The structural thesis is intact, but the asymmetry that justified the original sizing has collapsed.

## 4.2 Conviction calibration: two qualitative numbers

I am retiring the single-number conviction framing from prior memos. Conviction now has two components:

**Structural thesis confidence: high.** The memory upcycle is supported by LTAs across all three suppliers, the packaging bottleneck remains binding, hyperscaler capex revisions came in with direct memory attribution, and the Q1 2026 pricing data confirmed the supply-demand imbalance is not narrowing. The thesis has more structural support today than it did in March.

**Risk-adjusted position confidence at current price: lower.** The skew has collapsed to 1.1-to-1. The bear case implied move is now -63% rather than -23%. The base case is essentially flat from current. The structural thesis being right does not mean the position is right at $747. The two are different judgments, and the recommendation flows from the second number, not the first.

I am using qualitative descriptors here ("high" and "lower") rather than percentages on purpose. The single-binary conviction framework problem was that one number invited overconfidence and false precision. Two qualitative numbers reflect the actual structure of the decision: a strong structural view paired with a worse entry point.

## 4.3 Position sizing for new capital

The March memo recommended 5% portfolio sizing for the SK hynix / Micron pair based on an 11-to-1 upside-to-downside skew, calibrated through a Kelly Criterion framework. The current 1.1-to-1 skew implies smaller sizing on the same framework. Strict Kelly arithmetic would imply 1 to 2%. Recommending 2 to 3% is a deliberate compromise: it acknowledges the structural conviction is high, while honoring that the asymmetry no longer justifies the original sizing.

For readers: I would not deploy new capital at the original 5% size at $747. I would deploy at 2 to 3%, accept that the upside is more compressed than in March, and rely on the trim ladder below for downside discipline.

## 4.4 Trim ladder with arithmetic specified

The price-based trim ladder structure is unchanged from v1 in concept. The arithmetic is now explicit:

| Trigger | Action | Reasoning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $850 | First trim: 20% of original position | Above scenario-weighted target of $853 |
| $1,000 | Second trim: 30% of remaining position | DA Davidson Street high; bull case essentially fully priced |
| $1,150 | Third trim: 40% of remaining position | Stretch bull territory; requires cycle peak signals to not be flashing |
| $1,358.50 | Fourth trim: 50% of remaining position | Full bull scenario implied price; maintain residual as upside lottery |

The compounding math: at the full bull case, approximately 16.8% of the original position remains. This is intentional. The residual is a lottery ticket, not a clean exit. If the position is held through the full ladder, the residual still participates if Micron continues higher beyond $1,358.50, but the bulk of capital has been redeployed.

## 4.5 Cycle peak signals with mechanical thresholds

Six signals, each with a specific threshold and a specific data source:

**Signal 1 (inventory weeks):** DRAM supplier inventory above 15 weeks per TrendForce DRAM bulletins or Micron Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) at quarterly earnings exceeding 130 days. Triggering action: 25% trim of remaining position.

**Signal 2 (spot ASPs decelerating):** Three consecutive months of zero or negative month-over-month spot Average Selling Price (ASP) change per TrendForce monthly data, with contract prices still positive (the divergence is the signal).

**Signal 3 (industry capex on cleanroom):** 25% YoY increase in combined Applied Materials, Lam Research, and Tokyo Electron order book with explicit cleanroom-specific commentary on earnings calls. The shift from process and packaging capex to greenfield cleanroom is the indicator.

**Signal 4 (Wafer Fab Equipment (WFE) order cancellations):** Either a named major memory customer cancellation announced on a WFE earnings call, or aggregate book-to-bill below 0.9 at top three WFE companies for two consecutive quarters.

**Signal 5 (Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) de-specification):** Apple, Samsung, or Xiaomi announcing reduced DRAM specifications versus prior generation. Acknowledge this is a lagging signal because OEM de-spec decisions follow component cost pressures by 2 to 4 quarters.

**Signal 6 (peer gross margin and FCF plateau):** Two consecutive quarters of SK Hynix or Samsung operating margin declining QoQ. If both suppliers show the pattern, this is the cleanest peer signal.

## 4.6 Signal-based overlay arithmetic

The signals modify the price ladder. "Pull forward" means lower the trim price level by the percentage indicated.

**0 to 1 of 6 signals flashing:** Follow the price ladder as written.

**2 of 6 signals flashing:** Pull forward each trim threshold by 15% (lower price level). Worked example: $850 becomes $722.50 ($850 × 0.85). If 2 signals are already flashing at current price, the $722.50 trigger is already below the current $747, so an immediate 20% trim triggers.

**3 of 6 signals flashing:** Pull forward by 30%. $850 becomes $595. Plus consider full exit if both Signal 1 (inventory weeks rising) and Signal 2 (ASP plateauing) are among those flashing, since those two together indicate the cycle is rolling.

**4+ of 6 signals flashing:** Full exit regardless of price level. The structural thesis has broken.

The interaction language is deliberate: the signals do not replace the price ladder. They compress the price thresholds downward as a function of how many signals are firing. The intent is that the discipline gets faster as the cycle evidence accumulates.

## 4.7 Hard stop with size specified

**Trigger:** 20% drawdown from any new local high after May 10, 2026. If MU goes from $747 to $895 and then back to $716, the 20% drawdown from $895 triggers.

**Partial exit size:** 25% of the remaining position (not original position; remaining at time of trigger).

**Ladder reset:** The ladder resets at the new local high after the hard-stop pullback. If after the partial exit the stock recovers to $850, the first ladder trigger at $850 (20% of original) is treated as having already been executed by the hard stop. The ladder picks up at $1,000 (the next trigger).

## 4.8 Tariff rebalancing matrix

All trims are "of remaining position" (cumulative with prior trims). The discipline compounds. Triggering event: any announced memory tariff at the July 1 Commerce report, July 24 Section 122 expiry, or Section 301 conclusions.

| July 1 / July 24 / Section 301 Outcome | Position Action |
| --- | --- |
| No memory tariff (consistent with current trajectory) | Maintain (per ladder above) |
| 5 to 15% memory tariff | Trim 25% of remaining position on first trading day after announcement |
| 15 to 25% moderate memory tariff | Trim 50% of remaining position on first trading day |
| Over 25% aggressive memory tariff | Trim 75% of remaining position on first trading day |

## 4.9 Sideways case

The trim ladder is one-directional and assumes the price moves up. If the price stays flat or trades sideways for 12 months from publication of this memo (May 11, 2026 to May 11, 2027) with no ladder threshold hit and no signals flashing, the framework should be re-evaluated. The framework has a one-directional design and a sideways outcome is its blind spot. I am flagging it explicitly rather than papering it over.

## 4.10 Re-entry

This memo is exit-only. The trim ladder, signal overlay, and hard stop together describe how to reduce the position. They do not describe how to re-enter after a full exit. Re-entry will be addressed in a separate update if and when the position is fully exited.

The reason I am not specifying re-entry here is that the re-entry conditional depends on the exit conditional, which depends on which combination of signals or thresholds caused the exit. A full exit triggered by 4 of 6 signals (cycle break) has different re-entry logic than a full exit triggered by the price ladder reaching $1,358.50 (structural success). Specifying re-entry mechanics in advance would risk over-fitting to scenarios that have not happened.
