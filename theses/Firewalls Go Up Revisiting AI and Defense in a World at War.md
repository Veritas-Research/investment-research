# Why I'm Revisiting This

It started as a game.

In January 2026, a group of friends decided to run a stock-picking competition. Pick your top three names for the year, track performance publicly, and whoever performs best by December gets bragging rights. I took it more seriously than I should have. I spent a weekend building a full investment thesis - published on GitHub - arguing that the intersection of AI and defense cybersecurity was where the next decade of value creation would happen. I picked Zscaler, Snowflake, and UiPath.

Then a few of my friends read it.

One particular friend is not a passive reader. He's the kind of person who sends you a 2,000-word challenge document instead of just saying "good thesis." His core pushback: I was calling all three stocks "defense plays" but only one of them - Zscaler - actually had the government contract footprint to justify that framing. SNOW and PATH were AI plays with some government exposure, not defense investments. He also asked the pointed question I had avoided: if I'm making a defense + cybersecurity argument, why wasn't CrowdStrike in the portfolio?

It was a fair challenge. And it forced me to be more intellectually honest than the original draft had been.

I spent the next several weeks digging through FedRAMP authorization stacks, Impact Level certifications, DoD contracting databases, earnings transcripts, and government spending databases. The research is documented in a companion paper. The conclusions forced me to restructure the thesis entirely.

Then, on February 28, 2026, the world made the thesis academic no more.

US and Israeli forces launched a devastating aerial campaign against Iran, striking military sites and nuclear facilities across at least 26 of Iran's 31 provinces. Supreme Leader Khamenei was reportedly killed (still formally unconfirmed). Iran retaliated with missile and drone strikes - not just against Israel, but against all six GCC countries simultaneously. Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE were all struck. An entire region's "security illusion," as analysts described it, collapsed overnight.

And within hours, the cyber component of the conflict began. Iranian state-sponsored groups - the same ones CrowdStrike has documented and tracked for years - accelerated operations against US infrastructure. CRWD publicly linked recent attacks to operations originating in Tehran and its proxy networks, including Russian hacker group Z-Pentest.

Suddenly, this thesis wasn't about stock-picking games or competitive portfolio returns. It was about whether I had the right framework to understand a world that had genuinely changed.

This document is the answer to that question. It is longer, more quantitative, and more honest than version one. It adds CrowdStrike as a fourth position. It corrects the framing on Snowflake and UiPath. And it directly addresses what it would take to prove me wrong.

Mark's credibility is on the line here. So is the intellectual honesty that makes any investment framework worth reading.

# The Geopolitical Catalyst: February 28, 2026

To understand why this thesis matters right now, you need to understand what changed in the world - not just in the market.

## The Iran War and Its Cyber Implications

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a coordinated aerial campaign against Iran. According to reports, the campaign targeted military installations and nuclear development facilities across the country, hitting assets in at least 26 of Iran's 31 provinces ([Reuters, February 2026](https://www.reuters.com/)). US naval forces reportedly destroyed 17 Iranian warships including one submarine, according to early reporting (note: this figure is based on initial conflict reporting and total counts may have been revised as the conflict evolved; later sources cite varying figures). The strikes were described as among the most extensive in the region since the Gulf War.

Iran's retaliation was notable for one critical reason: for the first time, Tehran directed strikes not just against Israel but against all six Gulf Cooperation Council member states simultaneously. Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE were all targeted in the same attack window. Analysts who study Gulf security dynamics described it as the collapse of the premise that economic interdependence with Iran would protect GCC states from direct military engagement.

Estimated casualties exceeded 2,400 including significant civilian deaths. IRGC hardline elements consolidated decision-making authority as the conflict progressed.

Here is what this means for the investment thesis in concrete terms:

**Iran's cyber warfare infrastructure is now fully mobilized.** CrowdStrike has documented Iranian state-sponsored threat groups - most prominently Refined Kitten (also known as APT33) and Charming Kitten (APT35) - for over a decade. These groups conduct long-term persistent intrusions against US defense contractors, critical infrastructure, financial institutions, and government networks. In the aftermath of February 28, CRWD has publicly linked recent attacks to operations originating in Tehran and its allies, including Russian group Z-Pentest ([Motley Fool, March 18, 2026](https://www.fool.com/)). These are not theoretical threats.

**Zero Trust network security is now a wartime procurement priority, not a compliance checkbox.** When the US government is actively engaged in kinetic conflict with a state that has a mature cyber warfare program, the urgency of replacing legacy perimeter-based security architectures accelerates. Zscaler's argument - that traditional VPN-based network access creates exploitable seams that adversary nation states can penetrate - is no longer a sales deck. It is current events.

**The Intelligence Community's need for data infrastructure under hostile conditions is immediate.** Snowflake's AWS Intelligence Community Marketplace listing, achieved November 2025, now sits in a different context. IC agencies running operations against Iranian networks need data platforms that keep intelligence on US soil, under US legal jurisdiction, with no foreign cloud-act exposure.

## The Global Defense Spending Surge

The Iran conflict is the sharpest manifestation of a trend that predates February 28. The world has been rearming for years.

Global defense spending reached **$2.63 trillion in 2025**, up 2.5% year-over-year according to the IISS Military Balance ([IISS Military Balance 2026](https://www.iiss.org/)). The US proposed a record FY2026 military budget of **$1.01 trillion** - a 13.4% increase and the first time the US military budget has crossed the $1 trillion threshold.

Within that budget, **cyber spending is the fastest-growing line item:**

- Pentagon FY2026 IT/Cyberspace Activities budget: **$66.1 billion**, including **$14.3 billion in dedicated cyber investments** ([FY2026 IT/CA Budget](https://dodcio.defense.gov/))
- 2026 NDAA cyber budget: **approximately $15.1-15.5 billion** (4% increase), including $9.1 billion for core cyber operations and $612 million for research ([DefenseScoop](https://defensescoop.com/))
- DoD Zero Trust Strategy mandates all components reach target ZT levels by FY2027, with advanced certification by FY2032

The allied spending picture is similarly bullish for the sector:

- All NATO member states are on track to meet or surpass the 2% GDP defense spending target for the first time in the alliance's history
- Japan is committing defense expenditure to 2% of GDP by March 2026
- Taiwan has proposed reaching 5% of GDP by 2030
- South Korea agreed to an 8.2% defense spending increase in 2026

This is not a one-country, one-cycle story. This is a structural rearmament across the entire Western alliance and Pacific partners simultaneously. The [global defense AI market is projected to reach $22.75 billion by 2029](https://www.marketsandmarkets.com/), representing a compound growth rate that benefits every company in this portfolio.

# The Honest Reframing: What others Forced Me to Confront

Here is what the original thesis got right, what it overstated, and how the framing needs to change.

## What the Original Thesis Got Right

The macro thesis was sound. The convergence of AI infrastructure spending and defense/sovereignty requirements is real, well-funded, and multi-year. The original picks - Zscaler, Snowflake, and UiPath - were not mistakes. They are legitimately good businesses with meaningful government exposure.

Zscaler's defense positioning was accurately described, if anything underestimated. At the time of the January thesis, ZS had IL5 authorization, was trusted by 14 of 15 cabinet-level federal agencies, and was pursuing IL6 SECRET cloud via its SOCOM CRADA. That has only gotten stronger.

The Snowflake thesis around AI data infrastructure was correctly identified as a secular growth story.

The UiPath thesis as a contrarian value play at a compressed multiple with agentic AI optionality was a legitimate framing.

## What the Original Thesis Overstated

The uniform "AI + Defense" framing for all three positions was intellectually lazy.

Calling UiPath a "defense play" because the IRS and Air Force use it for workflow automation is like calling your accounting software a defense contractor because the Pentagon uses Excel. The government is a customer. That does not make you a defense company. UiPath's FedRAMP authorization is Moderate level - it covers unclassified civilian agency workloads. It has no IL4 or IL5 certifications. It is not cleared for DoD operational systems or intelligence workloads. The thesis for PATH is agentic AI platform value, full stop. Government adoption validates the commercial thesis; it is not the thesis.

Snowflake's defense angle was real but was presented as more immediate than it is. As of March 2026, SNOW's government revenue is a small but growing percentage of total. The IL5 authorization (April 2025), FedRAMP High on Azure Government (July 2025), and Intelligence Community Marketplace listing (November 2025) are genuine milestones - but they represent investment-phase costs that will convert to material revenue in FY2027 and FY2028, not today. The honest framing is "sovereignty/AI infrastructure play with a genuine and accelerating defense option" - not a peer to Zscaler's defense positioning today.

The most important omission was CrowdStrike. A bunch of people was right to call it out. If the thesis is genuinely about AI-powered cybersecurity as a defense infrastructure play, and if ZS addresses the network/access layer, then the obvious complement is the endpoint/detection/response layer - which is CrowdStrike's domain. The two companies are not competitors in any meaningful operational sense. They are frequently deployed together. A complete Zero Trust architecture needs both.

## The Corrected Position Framing

| Company | Honest Investment Framing | Defense Conviction |
|---------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| ZS | Conviction defense/cybersecurity play - deepest DoD ZT integration, only SASE/TIC 3.0 at FedRAMP High, IL6 in progress | Very High |
| CRWD | Conviction defense/cybersecurity play - endpoint + AI SOC layer, Charlotte AI FedRAMP High, Iran cyber threat vector | Very High |
| SNOW | Sovereignty/AI data infrastructure - IL5 achieved, ICMP listed, EU sovereign cloud partner, defense option materializing FY27-28 | Medium-High |
| PATH | Contrarian agentic AI value - compressed multiple, GAAP profitable, government as tailwind not thesis | Medium |

This is a more honest portfolio. It acknowledges that two positions are genuine defense plays, one is an AI infrastructure story with a growing defense dimension, and one is a commercial AI value trade. Each has a different thesis, a different time horizon, and a different risk profile. That portfolio construction is actually stronger than three positions with the same story.

## Pick One: Zscaler (ZS) - The Core Defense Conviction

**Current Price:** $155.99 (March 18, 2026)

**Market Cap:** $25.08 billion

### What I'm Betting On

Zscaler is the purest play on the US government's Zero Trust mandate. This is not a marketing claim - it is a procurement reality.

The DoD's Zero Trust Strategy, published in 2022 and updated since, requires all military components to achieve "target" Zero Trust capability levels by FY2027 and "advanced" levels by FY2032. DISA's Thunderdome program - the DoD's flagship ZT implementation - achieved 152 of 152 Zero Trust outcomes in April 2025, two years ahead of its deadline ([DefenseScoop, April 2025](https://defensescoop.com/2025/04/02/disa-thunderdome-zero-trust-randy-resnick/)). The government is not talking about Zero Trust anymore. It is buying it.

Zscaler's specific moat within that procurement universe is substantial:

**Zscaler Internet Access (ZIA) is, as of its August 2022 announcement and the most recent Zscaler DoD documentation (July 2025), the only SASE/TIC 3.0 solution with FedRAMP High Authorization** ([SDxCentral](https://www.sdxcentral.com/news/zscaler-internet-access-achieves-fedramp-high-authorization/)). TIC 3.0 is the federal standard for Trusted Internet Connections - the requirement that all federal internet traffic flow through approved, monitored access points. ZIA being the only SASE product at this authorization level is a procurement moat: agencies replacing legacy TIC infrastructure have essentially one vendor option at FedRAMP High.

**Zscaler Private Access (ZPA) holds IL2, IL4, and IL5 authorization, with IL6 SECRET cloud in progress via a CRADA with US Special Operations Command** ([Zscaler DoD White Paper](https://www.zscaler.com/resources/white-papers/secure-and-scalable-zero-trust-solutions-for-department-of-defense.pdf)). The IL6 program targets a fully functional SECRET-capable ZPA control and data plane within Air Force Cloud One. No other pure-play SASE vendor is as far along on IL6. When this is achieved, ZS becomes one of the very few commercial SaaS vendors operating at the classified workload level.

**14 of 15 cabinet-level federal agencies are Zscaler customers** ([Zscaler earnings Q2 FY2026](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/zscaler-announces-strong-second-quarter-210500187.html)). Federal spending with ZS has been growing at 70% year-over-year. The CEO has noted publicly that significant upsell opportunity remains, meaning the existing footprint is under-monetized relative to eventual contract value.

**The GDIT $120 million Air Force Next Generation Gateway zero trust task order (January 2026)** is a direct pipeline contract for ZS ([DefenseScoop, January 2026](https://defensescoop.com/2026/01/15/air-force-gdit-contract-zero-trust-bases/)). Systems integrators like GDIT win the prime contract and pull through vendors like Zscaler for the network security components. This is how most defense software gets bought - not directly but through SI channels.

### The Valuation Reset Is an Opportunity

The -51.7% decline from January entry has nothing to do with the thesis and everything to do with macro-driven multiple compression in high-multiple SaaS names. There was no earnings disaster, no major customer loss, no competitive displacement at the product level. The company grew revenue 23% year-over-year to $2.67 billion in FY2025 ([Zscaler Form 10-K FY2025](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/zscaler-annual-report-for-fiscal-year-ending-july-31-2025-form-10-k-ce7d59d2dd8df727)) and generates $727 million in free cash flow (27.2% FCF margin). ARR grew 25% year-over-year to $3.36 billion as of Q2 FY2026.

At $155.99, ZS trades at approximately 7.5x forward revenue (FY2026E of $3.32 billion). That is the cheapest ZS has traded on a revenue multiple basis in years, for a company with a deepening government moat, accelerating federal spending, and zero competitive displacement at the network security layer.

### Why CrowdStrike Belongs Here

Adding CRWD to this portfolio is the correction of what should have been obvious in January. The challenges sent to me made it explicit.

Zscaler and CrowdStrike occupy different layers of the same security architecture. ZS operates at the network/access layer - it secures how users and devices connect to applications and the internet. CRWD operates at the endpoint/detection/response layer - it secures what happens on devices once they're connected, and it identifies threats that have already entered the network. A complete Zero Trust architecture requires both. They are deployed together in virtually every mature government ZT implementation, not chosen between.

The question is not ZS vs. CRWD. The question is whether you want both, and at what weight. Given the Iran war catalyst, the answer is both.

### Why Today Specifically

March 18, 2026 - today - CrowdStrike announced a major expansion of its GovCloud platform at Fal.Con Gov 2026 in Washington DC. New capabilities added to the government cloud include:

- **Charlotte AI for Gov**: Agentic Response and natural language capabilities, the same AI SOC automation that Charlotte AI provides commercially, now available at government classification levels
- **Malware Sandbox for Gov**: Detonation environment for analyzing malware samples in the government cloud
- **External Attack Surface Management (EASM) for Gov**: Continuous monitoring of government agencies' internet-facing assets for vulnerabilities
- **Falcon for XIoT for Gov**: Securing operational technology, IoT, and connected device infrastructure in government environments
- **Falcon Flex for Gov**: Flexible licensing model that reduces procurement friction for government customers

This is not an incremental update. This is CRWD saying: we are making a serious, sustained push into the government market at the moment government customers have the most urgent need for endpoint security and AI-powered threat detection.

The backdrop matters. Charlotte AI achieved FedRAMP High Authorization in November 2025 - making it the first agentic AI SOC platform cleared at the government's highest unclassified level ([CrowdStrike Press Release](https://www.crowdstrike.com/en-us/press-releases/crowdstrike-charlotte-ai-achieves-fedramp-high-authorization-transforming-public-sector-defense-with-agentic-soc/)). Key capabilities include a Detection Triage Agent that analyzes alerts with greater than 98% accuracy and eliminates 40+ manual analyst hours per week per SOC. Government security teams - which are chronically understaffed and overwhelmed by alert volume - are the natural target buyers.

### The Iran War Is a Direct Catalyst

CrowdStrike is one of the world's leading threat intelligence organizations. Its Adversary Intelligence reports have documented Iranian state-sponsored cyber groups for over a decade:

- **Refined Kitten (APT33)**: Iranian group targeting aerospace, petrochemical, and energy sectors, particularly US defense contractors in the supply chain
- **Charming Kitten (APT35)**: Iranian group conducting long-term intrusions against government officials, think tanks, journalists, and defense-adjacent targets

Since February 28, CRWD has publicly linked recent cyberattacks against US entities to operations originating in Tehran and Iranian allied networks including Russian hacker group Z-Pentest. This is not theoretical. These are active intrusions that the customers CRWD protects are defending against right now.

Motley Fool named CRWD as a key Iran war beneficiary alongside Palantir on March 18, 2026 ([Motley Fool](https://www.fool.com/)). The logic is simple: when nation-state adversaries activate their cyber capabilities, the companies that detect, track, and stop those intrusions see demand accelerate.

# What Could Prove Me Wrong

I want to be specific here, not generic. "AI spending could slow" is not a risk analysis - it is a hedge. Here are the concrete scenarios that would force a thesis reassessment:

**1. AI capex cycle turns before enterprise adoption scales.** The entire thesis assumes that hyperscaler AI investment ($75+ billion annualized across Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) converts into enterprise AI production deployments that require ZS, CRWD, SNOW, and PATH infrastructure. If enterprises stay in "pilot mode" through 2027 because AI ROI is not measurable, the revenue growth rates for all four names disappoint, multiples compress, and the thesis is wrong. Early warning indicators: Snowflake consumption growth, CRWD's net new ARR from AI-adjacent products, PATH's ARR reacceleration.

**2. Microsoft wins the government security bundle.** Microsoft sells Teams, Office 365, Azure, and now Microsoft Defender/Sentinel/Entra as a unified government security stack - the E5 bundle at massive discount through the DEOS contract. If DoD and federal agencies consolidate onto Microsoft security products in the same way enterprises consolidated onto Teams, ZS and CRWD lose the procurement battles where Microsoft's bundling advantage is strongest. This is the single most credible bear case for the cyber names and it is not priced into current multiples.

**3. ZS IL6 timeline slips or does not materialize.** Zscaler's most powerful re-rating catalyst is achieving IL6 SECRET cloud authorization. If the SOCOM CRADA process takes until 2028 or beyond, or if the IL6 SaaS framework is deprioritized in favor of on-premises solutions, ZS loses its highest-ceiling defense moat.

**4. The Iran conflict ends fast and defense budgets re-normalize.** A rapid diplomatic settlement - enabled by regime change, ceasefire negotiations, or changed US political priorities - would remove the urgency premium from defense cyber spending. Defense budgets would still grow, but at the 2-3% baseline rather than the 13% step-change proposed for FY2026. The stocks would likely price out some geopolitical premium.

**5. PATH agentic AI thesis does not materialize by Q4 2026.** At 4.1x sales, PATH has limited downside protection if ARR growth decelerates further. If the Maestro platform fails to drive measurable enterprise adoption, and if Microsoft Power Automate or ServiceNow takes RPA/orchestration share, PATH could reach $8-9 on a compressed 2.5-3x revenue multiple. This would represent a -35% to -28% loss from current levels.

**6. SNOW government monetization timing disappoints.** If Snowflake's FY2027 earnings do not show material public sector revenue acceleration, the "sovereignty option" framing of this thesis looks premature. The market does not pay for optionality that does not convert to revenue within 24 months.

If I see two or more of the first three scenarios developing simultaneously, I would consider significantly reducing position sizes.
