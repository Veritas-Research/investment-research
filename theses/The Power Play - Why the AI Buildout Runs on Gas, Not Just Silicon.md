# Executive Summary (2-Minute Version)

**Context:** The entire market is paying for artificial intelligence (AI) one way: chips. NVIDIA is, as of early June 2026, the most valuable company on earth, the memory names re-rated, the data center builders re-rated, the power equipment makers re-rated. I went looking for the part of the AI buildout the crowd skipped, and I think I found it in an unglamorous place: the natural gas molecule that actually runs the data centers.

**What I Found:** The binding constraint on AI is no longer chips or buildings. It is power. Nearly 40 percent of data center projects in development are at risk of delay, and Goldman Sachs estimates only about 50 percent of capacity scheduled for the next two years will arrive on time, with lack of power the leading cause. Nuclear is years away, renewables cannot serve a 24/7 load, so natural gas is the only thing that scales inside the window. And United States gas is structurally cheap: in West Texas, Permian Basin associated gas went to a record negative $9.60 per million British thermal units (MMBtu) this spring, a local glut so severe that producers were paying to give gas away.

**What It Means:** Here is the part that took me a while to fully appreciate. Almost every way to play this has already re-rated. GE Vernova (the turbine maker) is up about 100 percent in a year. The nuclear utilities, the electrical suppliers, even the old crypto miners that pivoted to AI, all bid up. The corner of the AI chain the equity market has re-rated least is the gas producers. They are cheap. But there is a trap inside that, and avoiding it is the whole game.

**The Trap and the Play:** At roughly $3.50 gas, a generic gas producer does not work, because cheap, abundant supply caps the commodity no matter how strong demand gets. The producers that work better are the ones that sold part of their gas directly to data center power plants at a premium over the market price. My highest-conviction name is **EQT Corporation (EQT)**, which has signed about 1.4 billion cubic feet per day (Bcf/d) of firm, index-plus supply into Pennsylvania power projects. I want to be precise about what that does and does not protect: 1.4 Bcf/d is close to one-fifth of EQT's roughly 6.5 billion cubic feet equivalent per day (Bcfe/d) of production, so it locks a premium on a slice, not the whole company. My structure: EQT as the highest-conviction producer, Cheniere (LNG) as the steady, lowest-volatility anchor, Expand Energy (EXE) as a deliberate gas-price torque sleeve, and GE Vernova only on a pullback.

**Key Risk:** The biggest risk is not that the thesis is wrong. It is timing. If the AI trade de-rates, this whole basket falls with it for a while, regardless of gas demand. This is a bridge, built to work for the next one to four years until nuclear scales toward the end of the decade. Nuclear coming online at scale is the exit bell.

# Why I'm Writing This (and How I Built It)

I want to be transparent about the process, because the process is the point of this series.

I did not start with a stock. I did not even start the idea myself. The origin is Bob Brackett of Bernstein Research, who argued on The Real Eisman Playbook podcast that the AI power-demand story is really a call on natural gas, and named a handful of ways to play it. So this is not me discovering an ignored corner. It is me taking a known, publicly argued thesis and stress-testing it hard enough to decide whether I actually believe it, and which single expression survives.

To do that, I built a **spine**, a first-principles chain of logic: AI compute is committed, the buildings need power, power is the bottleneck, gas is the only bridge that scales, US gas is structurally cheap, so value should migrate to whoever owns the gas. Then I tested every link of that chain rather than every link I wanted to believe.

I ran a deliberately broad research sweep across demand, supply chain, valuation, and the bear case, and I ran each question through two independent AI research engines so I could see where they agreed (high confidence) and where they diverged (my job to adjudicate). That cross-check caught real errors, including one source claiming EQT produces 2.1 Bcfe/d when it actually produces over 6, a units mistake that would have wrecked the analysis. It also caught a subtler one in this very draft: an early estimate of Expand Energy's cash flow leaned on a quarter distorted by a January winter storm, and only a like-for-like comparison across the two engines surfaced it. Where a number actually drove a decision, I went to the primary source myself: I pulled Expand Energy's quarterly filing to confirm its cash flow, and I read EQT's own disclosures to confirm its data center deals.

The result is a thesis where I can point at any sentence and tell you where it came from, including the places where my own research argued against me. I will show you those places too, because a thesis that hides its own counter-evidence is not worth much.

Let's get into it.

# Part 1: The Bottleneck Nobody Priced

The consensus AI story is about silicon. I think the consensus is looking at the wrong shortage.

The chips exist. The buildings are going up. What is missing is electricity to run them. The evidence that power, not steel, is now the binding constraint is everywhere once you look:

| Signal | Figure | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Data center projects at risk of delay | Nearly 40% | Financial Times / SynMax, April 2026 |
| Scheduled capacity on track to arrive on time | As little as 50% | Goldman Sachs, May 2026 |
| North American data center vacancy | Record-low ~1% | JLL, year-end 2025 |
| Capacity under construction already precommitted | ~92% | JLL, year-end 2025 |
| PJM grid plant retirements by 2030 vs additions | 40 to 58 GW out, ~31 GW in | Grid filings, 2026 |

That last row is the one that matters most. The largest US grid operator, PJM Interconnection, is losing far more generation to retirements than it is adding, with reserve margins falling toward 5 percent. This is not a temporary queue backlog that clears itself. It is a structural shortfall in firm power that runs through the end of the decade.

One honest qualifier on "gas is the only thing that scales inside the window." The turbines that turn gas into electrons are themselves bottlenecked. Large-frame combined-cycle gas turbines (CCGTs, the big permanent power plants) are sold out into 2028 to 2030, and even the faster aeroderivative and reciprocating units only arrive quickly if a buyer holds a pre-reserved slot. So gas does not scale freely. What scales inside the next one to four years is behind-the-meter and pre-reserved capacity, which rewards incumbents who already hold gas, sites, and turbine slots. That constraint is a governor on the bridge, and it is also part of why the names with existing positions matter more than the commodity in the abstract.

Here is what told me this is real and not analyst hand-waving: follow the smart money. In the span of a year, the largest private capital firms on earth stopped buying data centers and started buying power. KKR launched a $10 billion venture, Helix Digital Infrastructure, with NVIDIA, Kuwait's sovereign wealth fund, and Texas power producer Vistra, built specifically to bypass the power bottleneck. Blackstone put more than $25 billion into a gas-fired joint venture with utility PPL. Brookfield committed $5 billion to gas-fed fuel cells with Bloom Energy. When KKR, Blackstone, and Brookfield all independently conclude that the way to win the AI buildout is to own the power, that tells me the demand is real.

It also tells me the theme is crowded, and I want to be honest about that rather than pretend I found something nobody sees. The distinction that still leaves an opening: that wave of private capital is buying the downstream power names (Vistra, PPL, Bloom), which have already re-rated. It is not buying the upstream gas producers. So the demand is validated by the capital, and the producers remain the part of the chain that capital has not bid up. Whether that is an opportunity or a fair price is exactly the question Part 3 takes on.

And the demand looks durable, which is the single most important rebuttal to the bear case. The fear has always been that AI gets more efficient and needs less power. Goldman's work projects a 24-times increase in token consumption by 2030 while explicitly assuming the cost per token falls 60 to 70 percent a year. In other words, on Goldman's numbers, even after pricing in radical efficiency, demand still goes up, because cheaper compute gets used more. I lean on this, but I will not oversell it: it is one bank's projection, and it depends on the assumption that usage grows faster than price falls. The near-term bear has real data on its side too, which I cover in Part 5. So I treat durable demand as well-supported, not settled.

The spine's first claim holds: power is the bottleneck, and it is structural.

# Part 2: The Gold-Rush Map (Why Most of the Chain Re-Rated and the Fuel Did Not)

Once you accept that power is the constraint, the obvious move is to buy the power. The problem is that everyone already did.

Think of the AI boom as a gold rush with distinct layers: the people selling picks and shovels (chips), the people building the mines (data centers), the people supplying the equipment (turbines, electricals), and the people who own the fuel (gas). "Re-rated" is just a polite word for what happens when the crowd shows up: they bid the stock up before the profits arrive, so you end up paying full price today for good news everyone already knows. Here is where each layer sits, with prices as of early June 2026:

| Tier | Representative names | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Chips and memory | NVIDIA (~$5T), Broadcom, AMD, Micron | Fully re-rated (Micron the lone cheap-on-forward outlier) |
| Builders, operators, electricals | Hyperscalers, CoreWeave, ex-crypto miners, Vertiv (~54x forward), Eaton | Aggressively re-rated |
| Turbines and nuclear power | GE Vernova (~40 to 45x forward, +100%), Constellation, Talen, Bloom Energy (+1,400%) | Already re-rated |
| **Gas commodity producers** | **EQT (~7.4x forward EV/EBITDA), Expand Energy (down ~18% on the year)** | **Least re-rated. The genuinely cheap corner** |

What struck me here was not that the power layer is cheap. It is that the power layer is mostly NOT cheap. The turbine maker is expensive. The nuclear utilities are expensive. Bloom Energy is up roughly 1,400 percent. The private capital wave I described in Part 1 is buying exactly these already-expensive names. The part the market has left on the discount rack is the upstream gas producer, the company that owns the molecule everything else depends on. Expand Energy, the largest gas producer in the country, is actually down about 18 percent over the last year while the gas it sells has never been more strategically important.

I will quote EQT's multiple with its basis, because cheap is a claim a reader should be able to check: about 7.4 times forward enterprise value to earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization (EV/EBITDA), versus a lower trailing figure on a different basis. It already carries a modest AI premium relative to where it traded a year ago, so "least re-rated" is the honest label, not "untouched." The asymmetry is real but narrower than a clean "nobody owns this" story: the demand is visible and accelerating, it is fully priced into the turbines and the utilities, and it is only partly priced into the fuel.

# Part 3: The Trap in Plain Sight

Here is where most people would stop, buy a basket of cheap gas producers, and feel clever. I think that is a trap, and understanding why is the most important part of this whole thesis.

The trap is this: the same cheap gas that powers the demand story also caps the gas producers' stocks.

Look at the price. The latest Energy Information Administration (EIA) outlook puts Henry Hub natural gas (the US benchmark price) at about $3.50 in 2026 and $3.46 in 2027, and the forward curve only reaches about $4.30 by late 2027. The consensus has been drifting down, not up. Why so cheap when demand is booming? Because the United States is drowning in gas. In West Texas, Permian Basin associated gas, a byproduct of oil drilling, is so oversupplied relative to pipeline capacity that the Waha hub traded at a record negative $9.60 per MMBtu this spring and stayed negative for 47 straight days. Producers there were paying to give it away.

A clarification I owe you, because it matters for the names later. That negative $9.60 is a local West Texas event, driven by Permian pipeline constraints. It is the single most dramatic illustration of US gas abundance, but it is not the price the producers in this thesis receive. EQT sells Appalachian gas priced off the Marcellus, which is cheap but does not see Waha's negative prints. I use Waha to make one point only: supply is so abundant that it caps the commodity nationally. I am not implying EQT sells gas at negative prices.

For a normal gas producer, that cap is a problem you cannot escape. If you just pump gas and sell it at the market price, then no matter how much AI demand grows, your stock stays cheap as long as the commodity stays cheap. Demand being right does not make you money.

So the question that actually matters is not "is gas demand going up." It is "who gets paid more than the market price, and on how much of their gas." That narrows the field dramatically, and it is what Part 4 is about.

# Part 4: The Way Through

There are only a few ways to own this thesis that survive a $3.50 gas price. They are the players who either lock in a price above the market, earn a fixed fee regardless of the gas price, or deliberately profit when gas rises.

## The Highest-Conviction Producer: EQT Corporation (EQT)

EQT did the one thing that softens the trap. Instead of selling all of its gas onto the open market at the cheap price, it signed long-term contracts to supply gas directly to data center power plants at an "index-plus" price, the local market rate plus a premium. Across two Pennsylvania projects, Homer City and Shippingport, it has locked in about 1.4 Bcf/d of firm supply starting in 2027. I verified this against EQT's own disclosures and trade reporting: the contracts are signed and priced on a Marcellus index-plus, or "M2-plus," basis.

I want to be precise about what index-plus does, because it is easy to overstate. It is not a fixed floor. It is the local index price plus a premium, so EQT still cares where gas trades, it just always earns a spread above it. The right analogy is a dairy farmer who sells 20 of his 100 gallons a day on a "market plus fifty cents" contract. He still feels it when milk gets cheap, because 80 gallons sell at the going rate and the protected 20 are themselves priced off the market. The 50-cent premium guarantees he beats the local price on that slice, not that he is immune to the price. EQT is that farmer. The 1.4 Bcf/d under contract is close to one-fifth of its roughly 6.5 Bcfe/d of production; the other four-fifths is ordinary price-exposed gas, and EQT is only lightly hedged on it (about 15 to 16 percent of 2026 volume, at a floor near $3.57).

So EQT is best understood as a low-cost producer with a protected premium corner, which still leaves it mostly a bet on the gas price. One caveat on sourcing: the headline 1.4 Bcf/d and the index-plus structure are well-corroborated, but the exact size of the premium rests on a single source, so treat the magnitude of the spread as directional. What makes EQT my highest-conviction producer is the combination: a protected slice, the lowest cash cost in its basin (all-in cash cost roughly $1.09 to $1.30 per thousand cubic feet equivalent), and a stock still at a single-digit forward multiple. It is the producer I would most want to own, not the lowest-risk name in the stack. That title belongs to the next one.

## The Steady Anchor: Cheniere Energy (LNG)

Cheniere liquefies US gas and ships it overseas, getting paid a fixed fee under 15-to-20-year take-or-pay contracts covering about 95 percent of its capacity. Think of it as a toll booth: it collects the toll whether gas is cheap or expensive. On its contracted volumes it is insulated from the gas price rather than a bet on it, with incremental upside on its own and spot volumes. It trades at roughly an 8 percent free cash flow yield, well above its midstream peers. This is the lowest-volatility way to own the theme, and it is the closest thing here to a sleep-at-night holding. Worth naming plainly: Cheniere is less an AI-demand bet than a structural "the world wants cheap, reliable US gas" bet, which is a feature. It does not depend on the AI story going perfectly, and global energy-security demand reinforces it regardless.

## The Torque Sleeve: Expand Energy (EXE)

Expand is the deliberate opposite of EQT. It is the largest US gas producer, it does not have the premium direct-supply contracts, and it barely hedges, so it is a nearly pure bet on the gas price itself. I am not pretending the market has it wrong. A barely-hedged price-taker should be cheap when the forward curve says gas stays soft, and that is exactly why Expand is down about 18 percent on the year. I would own it, in small and deliberate size, only as an explicit bet that gas firms above the consensus strip.

The math shows why it is a sleeve and not a core holding. I pulled its quarterly filing and modeled both ends: at $5 gas it would generate roughly $20 per share of free cash flow, against a stock around $90, a spectacular yield. At the $3.50 base case it generates only about $4 to $6 per share, an ordinary mid-single-digit yield. Same wells, same company; the entire case is a bet on the gas price rising. (That $4 to $6 estimate holds the realized-price discount constant across scenarios, the only assumption consistent with the filing-verified $20 figure at $5 gas; an early draft that used a weather-distorted discount overstated it, which the two-engine cross-check caught.) If your gas view is no better than the curve, you should not own this at all.

## The Bottleneck Winner, on Weakness Only: GE Vernova (GEV)

GE Vernova is one of only three companies on earth that make large gas turbines, and its order book is sold out into 2028 to 2030 with a $163 billion backlog. It is a wonderful business. It is also up about 100 percent in a year and trades at 40 to 45 times forward earnings. The demand is already in the price. I would only accumulate it on a real pullback, not here.

Here is the stack in one view:

| Name | Ticker | Role | Why it works (or does not) at $3.50 gas | Valuation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| EQT | EQT | Highest-conviction producer | 1.4 Bcf/d signed index-plus (~1/5 of output); lowest cost; rest still price-exposed | ~$33B cap, ~7.4x forward EV/EBITDA |
| Cheniere | LNG | Steady anchor (lowest risk) | Fixed-fee, ~95% contracted; insulated from the commodity | ~8% free cash flow yield |
| Expand Energy | EXE | Torque sleeve (bull-case only) | Does not; ~$4 to $6/share FCF at $3.50, ~$20 at $5 | ~$22B cap, no AI premium |
| GE Vernova | GEV | Turbine (on pullback) | Decoupled from commodity, but already expensive | ~40 to 45x forward, +100% |

EV/EBITDA is enterprise value to earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization.

One name on the watch list, not in the stack. **Comstock Resources (CRK)** has announced the single biggest potential deal in the group, a 5.2 gigawatt gas hub in Texas that NextEra would build to serve data centers, supplied by up to 1 Bcf/d of Comstock gas. I verified it against Comstock's own release and the magnitude is real, but the same release says it is "subject to negotiation and execution of definitive documents," so it is a selection, not a signed contract, the gas does not flow until 2031, and Comstock carries more debt. A 2031 start sits past the back end of this bridge, so I am keeping it on the watch list rather than in the position. If definitive documents get signed, it becomes a live, high-torque option.

# Part 5: What Would Make Me Wrong

I size this thesis the way I do because I can see the ways it loses. There are two, on two different clocks.

**The near-term risk is timing, not the thesis.** The biggest danger is not that AI stops needing power. It is that the AI trade de-rates on a sentiment or efficiency scare, and the whole basket, including the cheap gas names, sells off together because they are owned by the same momentum funds. In a risk-off move these names tend to fall together regardless of their individual fundamentals, so diversifying across the chain does not save you on the day. And the bear is not hypothetical: in the past year Microsoft cancelled roughly 200 megawatts of leases, Meta cancelled about $4 billion of US leases, Amazon paused some leases, and Microsoft's own chief executive said publicly "there will be an overbuild." That is real evidence that some of the announced demand is softening at the edges, and it is the strongest card the near-term bear holds. The trigger I watch is a hyperscaler capital-expenditure guide-down of more than 15 percent or two consecutive quarters of cuts, earliest in the back half of 2026. This is a "right thesis, wrong entry price" risk, and the answer to it is patience on entries, not avoidance.

**The medium-term risk is that cheap gas stays cheap.** If Permian supply and a coming wave of liquefied natural gas export capacity keep Henry Hub capped, the producers without direct contracts go nowhere even as volumes grow. This is exactly why EQT, with its protected premium slice and lowest cost, sits above Expand, which needs the price to cooperate.

My rough probability weights over a three-year horizon: bull 42 percent, base 39 percent, bear 19 percent. I want to be careful here, because my own validation work cut two ways. The demand side corroborated strongly, which makes me more confident in the theme. But the same work showed the price-taker producers are riskier than I first assumed: 2027 gas is moderate not rising, EQT is barely hedged on most of its book, and the LNG glut and capex-overbuild risks are live. So the honest net is not "more bullish on everything." It is more confident in the theme and more selective within it, tilting toward contracted and decoupled exposure (Cheniere, GE Vernova on weakness, and EQT's protected slice) over pure price-takers. Past 2030 the bear case roughly doubles as nuclear and alternatives finally arrive, which is the whole reason I treat this as a bridge with a shelf life, not a forever hold.

| Risk | Probability | What it does | What to watch |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI capex de-rating | Medium | Whole basket sells off on sentiment, multiples compress | Hyperscaler capex guide-down >15% or two quarters of cuts; further lease cancellations |
| Gas stays structurally cheap | Medium | Price-taker producers go nowhere; favors EQT and Cheniere over Expand | Henry Hub failing to hold the low $3s; LNG glut |
| Nuclear arrives early | Low before 2029 | Removes the bridge rationale | Small modular reactor commercial dates; the exit bell |

The honest version of my conviction: high on the spine, high on the sub-sector, high on EQT as the best producer to own, highest of all on Cheniere as the safest expression, and most uncertain on timing the entry. That is why this reads as a framework, not a "buy now" alarm.

# What Comes Next

Three things I am watching between now and the next update:

1. **The next round of hyperscaler capital-expenditure guidance (third and fourth quarter 2026).** This is the single most important near-term tell. If guidance holds, the de-rating risk stays low and the gas demand pull stays intact. If it cracks, or if the lease cancellations spread, the whole complex re-rates first and asks questions later.

2. **EQT's direct-supply contracts converting to cash (2027).** Homer City and Shippingport are scheduled to start in 2027. Watching those volumes actually flow at the contracted premium is what turns the EQT thesis from a story into a number.

3. **The nuclear clock.** Constellation's restart of Three Mile Island to serve Microsoft is targeted for 2027, the leading edge of the nuclear wave. One restart is far too small to matter against 50 to 65 gigawatts of gas demand, so it does not end the bridge. The exit signal is when credible commercial nuclear for data centers scales toward the 2029 to 2030 window, not the first single-site restart. When that clock moves from "around 2030" toward "now," that is my signal to trim.

I said this series is about showing the work, not just the call. So here is the call, stated plainly: the crowd bought the picks, the shovels, and the turbines, and re-rated the fuel the least. Most of the fuel is a trap, because cheap gas caps price-takers. The way through is the part of the chain that beats the commodity: EQT's contracted premium slice plus lowest cost, Cheniere's fixed-fee toll booth, and GE Vernova on weakness, with Expand as a deliberate, small bet on gas rising. It is the corner of the AI buildout the equity market has re-rated least, owned with discipline rather than as a single clever ticker. I will update this publicly as the contracts convert and the capex prints land.
