# The System Cost of Renewables: EROI, China's Solar Tell, and Why the Buffered Number Is the Story

**Research note by Mark A (Veritas Research), June 12, 2026.** Market commentary, not a formal thesis. Filed under research-notes because this is a broad-market read, not a single-name call.

**Shareable link:** https://github.com/Veritas-Research/investment-research/blob/main/research-notes/

## One-Line Summary

Renewables are not a dead end, but the market keeps pricing solar and wind on their best-case number while ignoring the one that actually matters at grid scale. China, the country that owns solar outright, is now behaving exactly as the honest math says it should. That gap is the opportunity.

## Executive Summary

- **The frame:** Vaclav Smil, drawing on Weissbach and co-workers (2013), splits Energy Returned on Investment (EROI) into two numbers. **Unbuffered** EROI measures the source as if it feeds the grid whenever it happens to generate. **Buffered** EROI adds the energy cost of storage to make an intermittent source "firm," meaning it can deliver power on demand like a coal plant can.
- **The punchline from Figure 8.3:** the buffered-versus-unbuffered gap barely touches dispatchable power (nuclear 75/75, coal 30/30, gas 28/28, hydro 49/35) but it guts intermittent power. Solar photovoltaics (PV) in Germany collapse from 3.9 to **1.6**. Wind falls from 16 to **3.9**. An EROI near 1 means you spend almost as much energy building and firming the source as you ever get back.
- **The real-world test:** China is the cleanest live experiment we have, and in 2026 it is pulling back hard on its own solar while pouring concrete for record coal. The buffered number predicted this; the unbuffered number did not.
- **My position:** Measured skeptic. Renewables keep growing and have real roles, but the "solar is simply cheapest, full stop" narrative is an unbuffered-EROI illusion. The system cost is structurally underpriced, and the dispatchable backbone (nuclear, natural gas, hydro) plus the hardware that firms a variable grid is what the market is sleeping on.
- **Key risk to the view:** storage keeps getting cheaper. If batteries and grid flexibility close the buffered gap faster than expected, the skeptic case weakens. This is a "watch the buffered number trend," not a "renewables are finished" call.

## Main Points

### 1. The whole argument lives in one chart

Smil's Figure 8.3 is the most honest single picture in the energy debate. Here is the data:

| Energy Source | EROI Unbuffered | EROI Buffered |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Solar PV (Germany) | 3.9 | 1.6 |
| Biomass (corn) | 3.5 | 3.5 |
| Wind (E-66 turbine) | 16 | 3.9 |
| Concentrating solar (desert) | 19 | 9 |
| Combined cycle gas turbine | 28 | 28 |
| Coal | 30 | 30 |
| Hydroelectricity (medium) | 49 | 35 |
| Nuclear (pressurized water reactor) | 75 | 75 |

Conventional wisdom holds that an EROI above roughly 7 to 10 is the floor needed to sustain a modern industrial economy. On the buffered number, German solar (1.6) and corn biomass (3.5) sit below that floor, and wind (3.9) sits right at the edge. Every dispatchable source clears it with room to spare.

### 2. "Cheap" is measured on the wrong number

When someone says solar is the cheapest electricity in history, they are quoting something close to the unbuffered case: the panel at the moment the sun is shining. That is real, and it is genuinely cheap. But a grid does not run on "when the sun is shining." It runs on demand, at night, in winter, during a windless week. Firming intermittent power requires storage, overbuild, transmission, and balancing, and the energy cost of all of that is what drags solar from 3.9 to 1.6.

How is that buffered number actually built? Weissbach used **pumped-hydro storage as the baseline buffer**: you pump water uphill into a reservoir when the panels are overproducing and let it run back down through turbines when they are not, which converts intermittent solar into power you can dispatch on demand, just like a coal plant. But building that reservoir, running it, and eating the round-trip energy losses (you never get back all the energy you used pumping the water up) all cost energy, and that energy is subtracted from what the source returns. That subtraction is the entire gap between 3.9 and 1.6. The buffered number is simply the honest number once you insist the source actually be reliable.

Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE), the headline metric everyone cites, mostly ignores these system costs. The buffered EROI does not let you ignore them. That is why it is the policy-relevant and investment-relevant number.

This is the heart of why Smil emphasizes the buffered figure. His point is that you cannot evaluate an energy source the way you evaluate a single power plant; you have to evaluate the whole **system** it plugs into. A coal plant is more or less self-contained. A grid running mostly on intermittent solar and wind is not a plant, it is a sprawling machine of generation plus storage plus long-distance transmission plus balancing, and every piece of that machine has to be built and powered. Smil's blunt conclusion is that a genuine large-scale renewable transition would require "a profound spatial restructuring of the existing energy system," with major environmental and socioeconomic consequences. The cheap panel is the easy part. The restructuring around it is the cost almost no one is pricing.

### 3. China is the live experiment, and it just blinked

China owns solar end to end: roughly 1,000 gigawatts (GW) of annual manufacturing capacity against about 270 GW of global demand, and more than 80% of every manufacturing stage. If grid-scale solar were a free lunch, the country that makes it cheapest would deploy it without limit at home. Instead, in 2026:

- New installs fell off a cliff. March was down 56% year on year, April down 79%. A record roughly 315 GW installed in 2025 is forecast to drop toward as low as 180 GW this year, the first annual decline in seven years.
- More than 40 Chinese manufacturers have gone bust, been bought, or delisted, and the surviving giants are posting billions in losses with modules selling below cost.
- Solar curtailment (power generated but thrown away because the grid cannot absorb it) hit roughly 9% in early 2026, even after Beijing relaxed the ceiling from 5% to 10% in 2024.
- Meanwhile China is building record coal, with most newly permitted plants described as "regulation" or "support," meaning dispatchable backbone for a grid that is getting more variable.

The country flooding the world with panels is quietly reserving reliable, dispatchable power for its own industrial grid. That is the tell.

### 4. Two explanations, both partly right

There is an honest debate about why China's solar collapsed, and a research note should hold both sides.

The physics camp (Doomberg is the sharpest voice) says this is the buffered number made visible: once intermittent share swamps the dispatchable capacity that balances it, curtailment spikes and the returns on the next panel collapse. The policy camp (named analysts at the Lantau Group, Ember, and the Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air) says the collapse was triggered by a specific pricing reform (China's Document 136, which ended fixed feed-in tariffs in June 2025) plus a 2025 rush to install before the deadline, and that curtailment reflects grid inflexibility, not a hard physical ceiling.

My read: they are describing the same wall from opposite sides. China hit a real integration limit, which vindicates the narrow engineering point that intermittent share cannot grow indefinitely without massive and expensive firming. But this is a managed deceleration, not the death of solar. China still targets roughly 3,600 GW of wind and solar by 2035. The truth is "more measured," not "dead end."

### 5. Where this points for the broader market

If the system cost of intermittency is real and underpriced, the value is not in the panel. It is in everything that makes a variable grid actually work, and in the dispatchable sources whose EROI does not collapse when you ask them to be reliable.

- **Nuclear.** The only source on the chart that is 75 buffered and 75 unbuffered. Artificial-intelligence (AI) data-center demand is the catalyst that finally makes the math and the politics line up. This is the cleanest expression of the buffered-EROI thesis.
- **Natural gas.** The dispatchable bridge, and the literal backup that every solar-heavy grid leans on. Ties directly to my existing "power behind AI" angle (gas turbines, Permian Basin supply).
- **Grid and firming hardware.** Transmission, storage, synchronous condensers, grid-forming inverters. The picks-and-shovels of the buffered gap. The more solar a grid adds, the more of this it has to buy.
- **The thing to be skeptical of:** pure-play panel manufacturers competing on price into a structurally oversupplied, below-cost market. China has already shown what that endgame looks like.

This is commentary, not a buy list. But the direction of travel is that the market pays up for "cheap" intermittent generation and underpays for the dispatchable and firming layer that the buffered number says is doing the real work.

### 6. Where the view could be wrong

I am not calling renewables a dead end, and I am not predicting a crash in solar. Three honest counterpoints:

- **Storage is on a cost curve.** If batteries keep getting cheaper faster than expected, the buffered EROI gap narrows, and solar-plus-storage starts to clear the floor on its own. Watch that number, not the headline panel price.
- **The Weissbach figures are from 2013.** Panels, turbines, and storage have all improved since. The shape of the argument holds (buffered is much lower than unbuffered for intermittent sources) but the exact numbers deserve a refresh.
- **Policy and subsidy can hold a trend up far longer than the math says.** Same lesson as the SpaceX note: "overpriced" is not "falls tomorrow," and "energetically marginal" is not "going away next year."

## Closing Line

The market keeps quoting solar's best number and pretending the grid runs on sunshine. China, which makes that sunshine cheaper than anyone, just told you the buffered number is the one that pays. Bet on what firms the grid, not just what feeds it.
